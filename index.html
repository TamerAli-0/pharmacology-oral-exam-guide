I.1Digitalis Glycosides (Digoxin)

Definition/Intro

Cardiac glycosides derived from Digitalis plants; digoxin is the prototype inotropic agent used in heart failure and atrial arrhythmias.

Mechanism of action

  • Inhibits the Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase pump on cardiac myocytes → intracellular Na⁺ rises → Na⁺/Ca²⁺ exchanger reverses → intracellular Ca²⁺ rises → increased contractility (positive inotropy)
  • Increases vagal (parasympathetic) tone → slows AV conduction and heart rate (negative chronotropy/dromotropy)

Pharmacokinetics — Digoxin vs Digitoxin

Parameter Digoxin Digitoxin
Absorption 75% oral ~100% oral
Protein binding ~25% ~97%
Metabolism Minimal (renal excretion of unchanged drug) Extensive hepatic metabolism
Elimination Renal (dose-adjust in renal failure) Hepatic
Half-life 36–40 h 5–7 days
Therapeutic range 0.5–2 ng/mL (narrow TI) —

Therapeutic uses

  • Heart failure (systolic dysfunction) — improves symptoms, does NOT reduce mortality
  • Atrial fibrillation/flutter — rate control via AV nodal slowing
  • Supraventricular tachycardia

Adverse effects

  • Cardiac: any arrhythmia is possible — most characteristic are bidirectional VT, PAT with block, AV block, PVCs
  • GI: anorexia, nausea, vomiting, diarrhea (earliest signs of toxicity)
  • CNS: visual disturbances (yellow-green halos — xanthopsia), confusion, fatigue
  • Hypokalemia potentiates toxicity (digoxin and K⁺ compete for same binding site on the pump)
  • Treatment of toxicity: Digoxin-specific antibody fragments (Digibind/DigiFab), correct K⁺, lidocaine or phenytoin for arrhythmias; avoid cardioversion if possible

Oral exam pearls

  • Hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia, and hypercalcemia all increase digoxin toxicity
  • Quinidine, amiodarone, verapamil raise digoxin levels (displace from tissue binding or reduce renal clearance)
  • Digoxin has a very narrow therapeutic index
  • Digoxin improves symptoms in HF but has no mortality benefit
Say it aloud — 30–60 sec

Mechanism: Inhibits the Na/K-ATPase; sodium builds up inside, so the Na/Ca exchanger expels less calcium — intracellular calcium rises and the heart contracts more forcefully (positive inotropy). It also raises vagal tone, slowing AV conduction and heart rate. Pharmacokinetics: Digoxin is excreted unchanged by the kidneys, half-life ~36–40 h, narrow therapeutic index — reduce the dose in renal failure. Digitoxin is ~97% protein-bound, liver-metabolised, half-life 5–7 days. Therapeutic uses: Heart failure (improves symptoms, not survival) and rate control in atrial fibrillation/flutter. Adverse effects: Nausea and vomiting first, then yellow-green vision (xanthopsia) and any arrhythmia, classically bidirectional VT; hypokalaemia, hypomagnesaemia and hypercalcaemia worsen toxicity — reverse with digoxin-specific antibody fragments (Digibind).

I.2Carvedilol

Definition/Intro

Carvedilol is a third-generation, nonselective β-blocker with additional α₁-blocking and antioxidant properties.

Therapeutic uses

  • Heart failure (HFrEF) — one of three β-blockers proven to reduce mortality in HF (with metoprolol succinate and bisoprolol)
  • Hypertension — α₁ blockade provides vasodilation in addition to β-blockade
  • Post-MI cardioprotection
  • Portal hypertension (off-label)

Adverse effects

  • Hypotension and dizziness (due to combined α₁ and β blockade)
  • Bradycardia, fatigue
  • Bronchospasm (nonselective β₂ blockade — avoid in asthma)
  • Weight gain, cold extremities
  • Must be titrated slowly in HF patients (“start low, go slow”) — can worsen HF acutely before long-term benefit

Oral exam pearls

  • Unique among β-blockers: blocks β₁ + β₂ + α₁ receptors
  • One of only three β-blockers with mortality benefit in HF
  • Antioxidant activity may provide additional vascular protection
  • Contraindicated in decompensated HF, asthma, severe bradycardia
Say it aloud — 30–60 sec

Therapeutic uses: A non-selective beta-blocker that also blocks alpha-1, adding vasodilation. Used in heart failure — one of only three beta-blockers that reduce mortality (with bisoprolol and metoprolol succinate) — plus hypertension and post-MI. Adverse effects: Hypotension, dizziness, bradycardia, fatigue and bronchospasm (avoid in asthma); in heart failure it must be started low and titrated slowly because it can worsen failure acutely before helping.

I.3Nonselective β-Adrenergic Blockers

Definition/Intro

Agents that block both β₁ and β₂ adrenergic receptors; prototypes include propranolol, nadolol, timolol, and pindolol.

Pharmacological effects

  • Heart: decreased heart rate, contractility, conduction velocity, and cardiac output (β₁ blockade)
  • Vasculature: initial increase in peripheral resistance (unopposed α); with chronic use, PVR normalizes
  • Bronchi: bronchoconstriction (β₂ blockade) — dangerous in asthmatics
  • Metabolic: mask hypoglycemic symptoms (tachycardia, tremor) via β₂ blockade; may impair glycogenolysis
  • Eye: decreased aqueous humor production → reduced intraocular pressure (timolol)
  • Renin: decreased renin release from JG cells (β₁)

Therapeutic uses

  • Hypertension
  • Angina pectoris (reduce myocardial O₂ demand)
  • Arrhythmias (supraventricular tachycardias)
  • Heart failure (selected agents only)
  • Migraine prophylaxis (propranolol)
  • Essential tremor (propranolol)
  • Thyrotoxicosis/thyroid storm — symptomatic control
  • Glaucoma (timolol — topical)
  • Portal hypertension (propranolol, nadolol)
  • Performance anxiety/stage fright (propranolol)

Adverse effects

  • Bronchospasm — contraindicated in asthma/COPD (β₂ block)
  • Bradycardia, heart block, worsening of acute decompensated HF
  • Fatigue, exercise intolerance, cold extremities
  • Masking hypoglycemia in diabetics (sweating is preserved — it is sympathetic cholinergic)
  • CNS: depression, insomnia, vivid dreams (lipophilic agents like propranolol)
  • Rebound hypertension/tachycardia with abrupt withdrawal (receptor upregulation)
  • Sexual dysfunction
  • Hypertriglyceridemia, decreased HDL

Oral exam pearls

  • Propranolol is the prototype — lipophilic, extensive first-pass, crosses BBB (hence CNS effects)
  • Pindolol has intrinsic sympathomimetic activity (ISA) — less bradycardia at rest
  • Never stop β-blockers abruptly — taper to avoid rebound
  • β₂ blockade = bronchospasm + masked hypoglycemia — the two classic contraindication scenarios
Say it aloud — 30–60 sec

Pharmacological effects: Block β1 and β2. β1 block lowers heart rate, contractility, cardiac output and renin; β2 block causes bronchoconstriction, blunts glycogenolysis and masks hypoglycaemia; they also lower intraocular pressure. Propranolol is the lipophilic prototype. Therapeutic uses: Hypertension, angina, arrhythmias, migraine prophylaxis, essential tremor, thyrotoxicosis, glaucoma (timolol) and performance anxiety. Adverse effects: Bradycardia, heart block, fatigue, cold extremities, bronchospasm (avoid in asthma/COPD), masked hypoglycaemia in diabetics, CNS effects like depression, and rebound hypertension if stopped abruptly — always taper.

I.4Organic Nitrates

Definition/Intro

Vasodilator drugs that release nitric oxide (NO); include nitroglycerin (NTG), isosorbide dinitrate (ISDN), and isosorbide mononitrate (ISMN).

Mechanism of action

  • Organic nitrates are converted to nitric oxide (NO) in smooth muscle
  • NO activates guanylyl cyclase → increases cGMP → activates protein kinase G → dephosphorylation of myosin light chain → vascular smooth muscle relaxation
  • Predominantly venodilation at low doses (reduces preload); at higher doses also dilates arterioles (reduces afterload)
  • Coronary vasodilation — especially of large epicardial vessels and collaterals

Pharmacokinetics

Drug Route Onset Duration Notes
Nitroglycerin Sublingual, IV, transdermal, spray SL: 1–3 min SL: 25–30 min Extensive first-pass (not given orally for acute); SL bypasses first-pass
Isosorbide dinitrate Oral, SL Oral: 30 min 4–6 h Active metabolite = isosorbide mononitrate; significant first-pass
Isosorbide mononitrate Oral 30–60 min 6–8 h 100% bioavailability, no first-pass metabolism
  • All nitrates develop tolerance with continuous use → must provide a nitrate-free interval (10–12 hours, typically overnight)

Pharmacological effects

  • Decreased preload (venodilation → decreased venous return) — most important effect
  • Decreased afterload at higher doses
  • Decreased myocardial O₂ demand
  • Redistribution of coronary blood flow to ischemic subendocardium via collateral dilation
  • Reflex tachycardia (baroreceptor response to decreased BP — reason to combine with β-blockers)

Adverse effects

  • Headache (most common — due to meningeal vasodilation; often diminishes with continued use)
  • Orthostatic hypotension, dizziness, syncope
  • Reflex tachycardia (can worsen angina if not combined with β-blocker)
  • Flushing
  • Tolerance with continuous exposure
  • Methemoglobinemia (rare, with very high doses)
  • FATAL INTERACTION with PDE-5 inhibitors (sildenafil, tadalafil) — severe hypotension → absolutely contraindicated together

Oral exam pearls

  • Nitroglycerin sublingual is the gold standard for acute angina attacks
  • Tolerance is overcome by a nitrate-free interval (usually remove patch at night)
  • Combine with β-blockers to counteract reflex tachycardia
  • Never give with PDE-5 inhibitors — life-threatening hypotension
Say it aloud — 30–60 sec

Mechanism: Release nitric oxide, which activates guanylyl cyclase and raises cGMP, relaxing vascular smooth muscle — mainly veins, reducing preload and myocardial oxygen demand. Pharmacokinetics: Sublingual nitroglycerin acts in 1–3 min and bypasses first-pass; isosorbide dinitrate/mononitrate are oral (mononitrate has 100% bioavailability). Tolerance develops with continuous use, so allow a nitrate-free interval. Pharmacological effects: Relieve angina, dilate coronaries and redistribute flow to the ischaemic subendocardium. Adverse effects: Throbbing headache (commonest), orthostatic hypotension, flushing and reflex tachycardia (combine with a beta-blocker); never with PDE-5 inhibitors like sildenafil — fatal hypotension.

I.5β-Adrenergic Blockers for Angina

Definition/Intro

β-blockers are first-line therapy for chronic stable angina; they reduce myocardial oxygen demand by decreasing heart rate, contractility, and blood pressure.

Pharmacological effects

  • Decreased heart rate (negative chronotropy) → prolongs diastolic filling time → improves coronary perfusion
  • Decreased contractility (negative inotropy) → less O₂ consumption
  • Decreased blood pressure → reduced afterload → less wall stress
  • Net effect: reduced myocardial O₂ demand → anti-anginal
  • Note: β-blockers increase coronary vascular resistance slightly (by allowing unopposed α-mediated constriction), but the O₂ demand reduction far outweighs this

Therapeutic uses

  • Chronic stable angina — first-line; reduce frequency and severity of attacks
  • Unstable angina / NSTEMI — standard of care
  • Post-MI — reduce reinfarction and mortality
  • Not effective in vasospastic (Prinzmetal/variant) angina — may worsen spasm (unopposed α)

Adverse effects

  • Bradycardia, hypotension, fatigue
  • Bronchospasm (nonselective agents)
  • Cold extremities (peripheral vasoconstriction)
  • Masking of hypoglycemia
  • Worsening of decompensated HF acutely
  • Rebound angina with abrupt withdrawal
  • Contraindicated in vasospastic angina

Oral exam pearls

  • β-blockers are the only anti-anginal class proven to reduce mortality post-MI
  • Preferred agents: metoprolol, atenolol (β₁-selective) for angina
  • Combine with nitrates for synergy (nitrates handle reflex tachycardia issue; β-blockers handle nitrate-induced reflex tachycardia)
  • Never use alone in Prinzmetal angina — can worsen coronary spasm
Say it aloud — 30–60 sec

Pharmacological effects: Reduce myocardial oxygen demand by lowering heart rate, contractility and blood pressure; the slower rate also lengthens diastole and improves coronary perfusion. Therapeutic uses: First-line for chronic stable angina, standard in unstable angina/NSTEMI, and post-MI they reduce reinfarction and mortality; they combine well with nitrates. Adverse effects: Bradycardia, hypotension, fatigue, bronchospasm, cold extremities and masked hypoglycaemia. Do NOT use alone in vasospastic (Prinzmetal) angina — unopposed alpha worsens spasm; use a calcium channel blocker there.

I.6Calcium Channel Blockers (CCBs)

Definition/Intro

CCBs block L-type voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels in cardiac and smooth muscle, reducing contractility, heart rate, and vascular tone.

Classification

Class Drugs Primary action
Dihydropyridines (DHPs) Nifedipine, amlodipine, felodipine, nicardipine Vascular smooth muscle (vasodilation); minimal cardiac effects
Non-DHP — Phenylalkylamine Verapamil Cardiac (negative inotropy, chronotropy, dromotropy) + moderate vasodilation
Non-DHP — Benzothiazepine Diltiazem Intermediate — both cardiac and vascular effects

Mechanism of action (for angina)

  • Block L-type Ca²⁺ channels → decreased Ca²⁺ entry into vascular smooth muscle → coronary and peripheral vasodilation → decreased afterload
  • Non-DHPs (verapamil, diltiazem): also decrease heart rate and contractility → reduced myocardial O₂ demand
  • DHPs (nifedipine): primarily vasodilate → decrease afterload; may cause reflex tachycardia
  • Effective in vasospastic (Prinzmetal) angina — directly relax coronary smooth muscle

Pharmacological effects

  • Vasodilation → decreased BP, decreased afterload
  • Coronary vasodilation → relief of vasospasm
  • Negative inotropy, chronotropy, dromotropy (verapamil > diltiazem >> nifedipine)
  • DHPs cause reflex tachycardia (nifedipine, especially short-acting)

Therapeutic uses

  • Angina — all three types (stable, vasospastic, unstable)
  • Prinzmetal/vasospastic angina — CCBs are drug of choice
  • Hypertension (amlodipine widely used)
  • Supraventricular tachycardia (verapamil, diltiazem — AV nodal blockade)
  • Raynaud phenomenon (nifedipine)
  • Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (verapamil)

Adverse effects

  • DHPs (nifedipine): flushing, headache, dizziness, peripheral edema (arteriolar dilation), reflex tachycardia, gingival hyperplasia
  • Verapamil: constipation (most characteristic), bradycardia, AV block, worsening HF (negative inotropy), gingival hyperplasia
  • Diltiazem: bradycardia, AV block (less than verapamil), peripheral edema

Cardiac effects: Verapamil vs Nifedipine

Effect Verapamil Nifedipine
Heart rate Decreases (direct SA node suppression) Increases (reflex tachycardia)
Contractility Decreases (significant negative inotropy) Minimal direct effect
AV conduction Decreases (prolongs AV nodal refractory period) No significant effect
Vasodilation Moderate Potent
Use in arrhythmias Yes (SVT) No
HF risk Can worsen HF Less cardiac depression

Drug interactions of Verapamil

  • β-blockers — additive negative inotropy, chronotropy, dromotropy → risk of severe bradycardia, AV block, HF (DO NOT combine IV verapamil + IV β-blocker)
  • Digoxin — verapamil increases digoxin levels (reduces renal and extrarenal clearance) → digoxin toxicity
  • Statins (simvastatin) — verapamil inhibits CYP3A4 → increased statin levels → rhabdomyolysis risk
  • Carbamazepine, cyclosporine — verapamil increases their levels via CYP3A4 inhibition
  • Quinidine — additive hypotension
  • Grapefruit juice — inhibits intestinal CYP3A4 → increased verapamil bioavailability

Oral exam pearls

  • DHPs end in “-dipine” → think “dip in pressure” (vasodilators)
  • Verapamil’s most unique side effect: constipation
  • CCBs are the drug of choice for Prinzmetal (vasospastic) angina
  • Never give IV verapamil + IV β-blocker — risk of asystole
Say it aloud — 30–60 sec

Classification: Dihydropyridines (nifedipine, amlodipine) act on vessels; non-dihydropyridines verapamil and diltiazem act on the heart. Mechanism: Block L-type calcium channels — arterial vasodilation lowers afterload; verapamil/diltiazem also reduce heart rate and contractility. Uses: All types of angina (drug of choice in vasospastic), hypertension, and SVT (verapamil/diltiazem). Adverse effects: Nifedipine → flushing, headache, ankle oedema, reflex tachycardia; verapamil → constipation, bradycardia, AV block, worsening heart failure. Verapamil vs nifedipine: verapamil lowers heart rate, contractility and AV conduction, while nifedipine is a potent vasodilator causing reflex tachycardia. Verapamil interactions: never with a beta-blocker (severe bradycardia/asystole), and it raises digoxin levels.

I.7Ticlopidine, Clopidogrel, Prasugrel

Definition/Intro

Thienopyridine antiplatelet drugs that irreversibly block the P2Y₁₂ ADP receptor on platelets.

Mechanism of action

  • All are prodrugs requiring hepatic activation (CYP450-dependent, especially CYP2C19 for clopidogrel)
  • Active metabolite irreversibly binds the P2Y₁₂ ADP receptor on platelets
  • Prevents ADP-mediated activation of the GPIIb/IIIa complex → inhibits platelet aggregation
  • Effect lasts for the lifetime of the platelet (~7–10 days)

Pharmacological effects

  • Irreversible inhibition of ADP-induced platelet aggregation
  • Prolonged bleeding time
  • No effect on prostaglandin pathway (unlike aspirin)

Therapeutic uses

  • Clopidogrel: acute coronary syndromes (ACS), post-PCI with stent (dual antiplatelet therapy with aspirin), stroke/TIA prevention, peripheral arterial disease; most widely used of the three
  • Prasugrel: ACS patients undergoing PCI — faster onset and more potent than clopidogrel
  • Ticlopidine: largely replaced by clopidogrel due to toxicity; sometimes used for stroke prevention when aspirin is contraindicated

Adverse effects

  • Ticlopidine: neutropenia/agranulocytosis (most serious — requires CBC monitoring), TTP (thrombotic thrombocytopenic purpura), GI upset, rash
  • Clopidogrel: bleeding, GI upset, rash, TTP (rare); CYP2C19 poor metabolizers have reduced drug activation → diminished efficacy; omeprazole inhibits CYP2C19 and may reduce clopidogrel’s effect
  • Prasugrel: higher bleeding risk than clopidogrel (including fatal bleeding); contraindicated in patients with prior stroke/TIA, age ≥75, or body weight <60 kg

Oral exam pearls

  • Clopidogrel is the most commonly used; prasugrel is more potent but has more bleeding
  • Ticlopidine = think neutropenia — that is why it was replaced
  • Clopidogrel + omeprazole interaction (CYP2C19) — use pantoprazole instead if PPI is needed
  • All are irreversible — must be stopped 5–7 days before elective surgery
Say it aloud — 30–60 sec

Mechanism: Prodrugs (activated by CYP450) that irreversibly block the platelet P2Y12 ADP receptor, stopping GPIIb/IIIa activation and aggregation for the platelet’s lifespan. Pharmacological effects: Irreversibly inhibit ADP-induced aggregation and prolong bleeding time. Therapeutic uses: Clopidogrel — ACS, after coronary stents (with aspirin), stroke/TIA prevention; prasugrel — ACS with PCI (faster, stronger); ticlopidine is largely abandoned. Adverse effects: Bleeding; ticlopidine causes neutropenia and TTP; prasugrel bleeds most (avoid after prior stroke); clopidogrel is weakened by omeprazole (CYP2C19). Stop 5–7 days before surgery.

I.8Heparin

Definition/Intro

Heparin (unfractionated, UFH) is a naturally occurring, rapidly acting parenteral anticoagulant; low-molecular-weight heparins (LMWHs) such as enoxaparin are derivatives with more predictable pharmacokinetics.

Mechanism of action

  • Binds antithrombin III (AT III) → enhances its activity 1000-fold
  • The heparin–AT III complex inactivates thrombin (IIa), Xa, and other serine proteases (IXa, XIa, XIIa)
  • UFH: inhibits both thrombin (IIa) and Xa equally (requires a long chain to bridge AT III to thrombin)
  • LMWH: preferentially inhibits Factor Xa (too short to bridge AT III to thrombin)

Pharmacological effects

  • Prolongs aPTT (activated partial thromboplastin time) — used to monitor UFH
  • LMWH: monitored by anti-Xa levels if needed (usually no routine monitoring)
  • Immediate anticoagulant effect (IV UFH: seconds; SC: 1–2 hours)
  • Does NOT cross placenta — safe in pregnancy

Therapeutic uses

  • Acute DVT and PE — initial treatment
  • Acute coronary syndromes (ACS)
  • Prevention of DVT (surgical prophylaxis — especially LMWH)
  • During cardiopulmonary bypass, hemodialysis, cardiac catheterization
  • Bridge therapy while initiating warfarin
  • Pregnancy-associated thromboembolism (does not cross placenta)

Adverse effects

  • Bleeding — most common; antidote is protamine sulfate (1 mg neutralizes ~100 U heparin; less effective for LMWH)
  • Heparin-induced thrombocytopenia (HIT)
  • Type I: mild, non-immune, transient — not clinically significant
  • Type II: immune-mediated (anti-PF4 antibodies) → paradoxical thrombosis (white clot syndrome) — stop heparin immediately; switch to direct thrombin inhibitor (argatroban, bivalirudin)
  • Osteoporosis (with long-term use — heparin activates osteoclasts)
  • Hyperkalemia (suppresses aldosterone synthesis)
  • No teratogenicity (advantage over warfarin)

Oral exam pearls

  • Monitor UFH with aPTT; monitor LMWH with anti-Xa (if needed)
  • Antidote: protamine sulfate
  • HIT Type II = think thrombosis, not just bleeding — most dangerous complication
  • Safe in pregnancy (does not cross placenta) — unlike warfarin
Say it aloud — 30–60 sec

Mechanism: Binds antithrombin III and boosts it ~1000-fold, inactivating thrombin (IIa) and factor Xa; LMWH (enoxaparin) mainly inhibits Xa. Pharmacological effects: Immediate anticoagulation, prolongs aPTT (used to monitor UFH), and does not cross the placenta. Therapeutic uses: Acute DVT and PE, ACS, DVT prophylaxis, bridging to warfarin, and anticoagulation in pregnancy. Adverse effects: Bleeding (reverse with protamine sulfate); HIT type II — an immune reaction that paradoxically causes thrombosis, so stop heparin and switch to a direct thrombin inhibitor; long-term use causes osteoporosis and hyperkalaemia.

I.9Warfarin

Definition/Intro

Warfarin is an oral anticoagulant that inhibits vitamin K-dependent clotting factor synthesis; it is the prototype oral anticoagulant with a narrow therapeutic index.

Mechanism of action

  • Inhibits vitamin K epoxide reductase (VKORC1) → prevents recycling of oxidized vitamin K to its reduced (active) form
  • Reduced vitamin K is essential for γ-carboxylation of clotting factors II, VII, IX, X and anticoagulant proteins C and S
  • Onset is delayed 3–5 days (must wait for already-synthesized factors to be depleted; factor VII has the shortest half-life ~6 h, but full effect requires depletion of factor II ~60 h)
  • Initial procoagulant phase: proteins C and S (short half-lives) decrease before factors II and X → transient hypercoagulable state → reason to overlap with heparin for first 5 days

Pharmacokinetics

  • Well absorbed orally (100% bioavailability)
  • Highly protein-bound (~99%) to albumin → many displacement interactions
  • Metabolized by hepatic CYP2C9, CYP3A4
  • Long half-life: ~36–42 hours (S-warfarin, the more potent enantiomer, via CYP2C9)
  • Monitored by PT/INR (target INR 2–3 for most indications; 2.5–3.5 for mechanical valves)
  • Crosses the placenta — teratogenic

Pharmacological effects

  • Decreased synthesis of functional factors II, VII, IX, X
  • Prolonged PT/INR
  • Full anticoagulant effect takes 3–5 days

Therapeutic uses

  • Long-term prevention of DVT/PE
  • Atrial fibrillation (stroke prevention)
  • Mechanical heart valves (prosthetic valve thromboprophylaxis)
  • Post-MI (in select patients)
  • Antiphospholipid syndrome

Adverse effects

  • Bleeding (most common) — antidote: vitamin K (phytonadione); for life-threatening bleeds: fresh frozen plasma (FFP) or 4-factor prothrombin complex concentrate (PCC) for immediate reversal
  • Warfarin-induced skin necrosis — occurs in first few days, especially in protein C-deficient patients; due to early drop in protein C → microvascular thrombosis in skin/fat → reason to bridge with heparin
  • Purple/blue toe syndrome (cholesterol microembolization)
  • Teratogenicity — nasal hypoplasia, stippled epiphyses (first trimester); CNS abnormalities (any trimester) — absolutely contraindicated in pregnancy

Drug interactions

  • Increase warfarin effect (↑ bleeding risk):
  • CYP2C9 inhibitors: metronidazole, fluconazole, amiodarone, sulfonamides
  • Displacement from albumin: NSAIDs, sulfonamides
  • Decreased vitamin K synthesis by gut flora: broad-spectrum antibiotics
  • Inhibit platelet function: aspirin, NSAIDs
  • Others: cimetidine, omeprazole, thyroid hormones
  • Decrease warfarin effect (↓ anticoagulation):
  • CYP inducers: rifampin (most potent), carbamazepine, phenytoin, barbiturates, St. John’s wort
  • Increased clotting factor synthesis: pregnancy, oral contraceptives
  • Vitamin K-rich foods (green leafy vegetables) — dietary competition
  • Cholestyramine — decreased warfarin absorption

Contraindications

  • Pregnancy (teratogenic — Category X)
  • Active bleeding, bleeding diatheses
  • Recent surgery (CNS, eye)
  • Severe hepatic/renal disease
  • Unsupervised patients (poor compliance risk given narrow TI)

Oral exam pearls

  • Delayed onset (3–5 days) — must bridge with heparin
  • Monitor with PT/INR, not aPTT
  • Warfarin skin necrosis = think protein C deficiency
  • Rifampin is the strongest enzyme inducer reducing warfarin effect; metronidazole/fluconazole are common CYP2C9 inhibitors that increase effect
Say it aloud — 30–60 sec

Mechanism: Inhibits vitamin K epoxide reductase, blocking gamma-carboxylation of factors II, VII, IX, X and proteins C and S. Pharmacokinetics: Oral, ~99% protein-bound, metabolised by CYP2C9; slow onset of 3–5 days (bridge with heparin); monitored by PT/INR (target 2–3). Pharmacological effects: Fewer functional clotting factors, prolonged INR. Therapeutic uses: Long-term prevention — atrial fibrillation, mechanical valves, DVT/PE. Adverse effects: Bleeding (reverse with vitamin K, or FFP/PCC in emergencies) and skin necrosis in protein C deficiency. Interactions: Inhibitors (metronidazole, fluconazole, amiodarone) raise effect; inducers (rifampin, carbamazepine) lower it. Contraindications: Pregnancy, active bleeding, severe liver disease.

I.11ACE Inhibitors

Definition/Intro

ACE inhibitors (e.g., captopril, enalapril, lisinopril, ramipril) block angiotensin-converting enzyme, reducing angiotensin II production; they end in “-pril”.

Mechanism of action

  • Inhibit angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE) → block conversion of angiotensin I to angiotensin II
  • Decreased angiotensin II → decreased aldosterone secretion → decreased Na⁺/H₂O retention
  • Decreased angiotensin II → decreased vasoconstriction → vasodilation (both arteriolar and venous)
  • ACE also degrades bradykinin → ACE inhibition causes bradykinin accumulation → additional vasodilation (via NO and prostacyclin) — also responsible for cough and angioedema
  • Decrease cardiac and vascular remodeling (long-term benefit in HF and post-MI)

Pharmacological effects

  • Decreased blood pressure (reduced PVR, no reflex tachycardia)
  • Decreased aldosterone → mild diuresis, K⁺ retention
  • Decreased preload and afterload
  • Decreased cardiac remodeling and hypertrophy
  • Renoprotective — dilate efferent arteriole → decrease intraglomerular pressure → slow progression of diabetic nephropathy

Therapeutic uses

  • Hypertension — first-line, especially in diabetics
  • Heart failure (HFrEF) — reduce mortality (cornerstone of HF therapy)
  • Post-MI — reduce remodeling and mortality (especially with LV dysfunction)
  • Diabetic nephropathy — renoprotective (first-line in DM with proteinuria)
  • Chronic kidney disease with proteinuria
  • Scleroderma renal crisis (drug of choice)

Adverse effects

  • Dry cough (10–20%) — due to bradykinin accumulation in lungs; if intolerable, switch to ARB
  • Angioedema (rare but life-threatening) — also from bradykinin; more common in Black patients
  • Hyperkalemia (decreased aldosterone) — avoid combining with K⁺-sparing diuretics or K⁺ supplements
  • Hypotension (first-dose effect, especially if volume-depleted)
  • Acute renal failure in bilateral renal artery stenosis (efferent arteriole dilation removes compensatory mechanism maintaining GFR)
  • Teratogenicity — contraindicated in pregnancy (fetal renal agenesis, oligohydramnios, skull defects)
  • Dysgeusia (taste disturbance — especially captopril)
  • Rash (captopril — contains sulfhydryl group)

Oral exam pearls

  • Cough = bradykinin → switch to ARB (no cough because ARBs do not affect bradykinin)
  • Bilateral renal artery stenosis = absolute contraindication
  • Contraindicated in pregnancy (all trimesters)
  • ACE inhibitors + ARBs together = increased adverse effects, no added benefit — avoid dual RAAS blockade
Say it aloud — 30–60 sec

Mechanism: Block angiotensin-converting enzyme, so less angiotensin II (vasodilation, less aldosterone), and they reduce breakdown of bradykinin. Pharmacological effects: Lower blood pressure without reflex tachycardia, reduce cardiac remodelling, and protect the kidney by dilating the efferent arteriole. Therapeutic uses: First-line in hypertension (especially diabetics), heart failure and post-MI (reduce mortality), and diabetic nephropathy. Adverse effects: Dry cough and angioedema (bradykinin), hyperkalaemia, first-dose hypotension, and acute kidney injury in bilateral renal artery stenosis; contraindicated in pregnancy.

I.12Angiotensin Receptor Blockers (ARBs)

Definition/Intro

ARBs (e.g., losartan, valsartan, candesartan, irbesartan) selectively block the AT₁ receptor for angiotensin II; they end in “-sartan”.

Mechanism of action

  • Selectively block the angiotensin II type 1 (AT₁) receptor on blood vessels, adrenal cortex, and heart
  • Prevent angiotensin II-mediated vasoconstriction, aldosterone release, and cardiac remodeling
  • Unlike ACE inhibitors, ARBs do NOT inhibit bradykinin degradation → no cough, much lower angioedema risk
  • Angiotensin II is still produced but cannot act on AT₁ → may stimulate AT₂ receptors (which may provide additional vasodilation and anti-proliferative effects)

Pharmacological effects

  • Decreased blood pressure (vasodilation, decreased aldosterone)
  • Decreased cardiac remodeling
  • Renoprotective (same efferent arteriole dilation as ACE inhibitors)
  • Mild K⁺ retention
  • No effect on bradykinin metabolism

Therapeutic uses

  • Hypertension — first-line alternative; drug of choice for patients who cannot tolerate ACE inhibitor cough
  • Heart failure (when ACE inhibitors not tolerated)
  • Diabetic nephropathy — renoprotective (especially irbesartan, losartan in type 2 DM)
  • Post-MI with LV dysfunction (valsartan)
  • Stroke prevention in hypertensive patients with LVH (losartan)

Adverse effects

  • Hyperkalemia (same as ACE inhibitors — decreased aldosterone)
  • Hypotension
  • Acute renal failure in bilateral renal artery stenosis (same mechanism as ACE inhibitors)
  • Teratogenicity — contraindicated in pregnancy (same as ACE inhibitors)
  • No dry cough (key advantage over ACE inhibitors)
  • Angioedema (very rare — much less than with ACE inhibitors, but still possible)

Oral exam pearls

  • ARBs = “ACE inhibitors without the cough”
  • Same contraindications as ACE inhibitors: pregnancy, bilateral renal artery stenosis, hyperkalemia
  • Do NOT combine ACE inhibitor + ARB — dual RAAS blockade increases hyperkalemia, renal failure, and hypotension without benefit
  • Losartan has unique uricosuric effect (lowers uric acid — useful in gout)
Say it aloud — 30–60 sec

Mechanism: Selectively block the angiotensin II AT1 receptor, preventing vasoconstriction, aldosterone release and remodelling; unlike ACE inhibitors they don’t affect bradykinin, so no cough. Pharmacological effects: Lower blood pressure and are renoprotective, like ACE inhibitors. Therapeutic uses: Hypertension, heart failure and diabetic nephropathy — the choice when a patient can’t tolerate the ACE inhibitor cough. Adverse effects: Hyperkalaemia, hypotension, and renal failure in bilateral renal artery stenosis; contraindicated in pregnancy. Losartan also lowers uric acid; never combine an ACE inhibitor with an ARB.

I.13Therapeutic Strategies for Treatment of Hypertension

Definition/Intro

Hypertension management follows a stepped approach starting with lifestyle modification and progressing to pharmacotherapy based on BP stage and comorbidities.

First-line drug classes (JNC guidelines / current recommendations)

  1. Thiazide diuretics (chlorthalidone, hydrochlorothiazide) — effective in most patients, especially elderly and Black patients
  2. ACE inhibitors (-prils) — preferred in diabetes, CKD, HF, post-MI
  3. ARBs (-sartans) — alternative to ACE inhibitors (if cough)
  4. Calcium channel blockers (amlodipine, nifedipine) — effective in elderly, Black patients, isolated systolic HTN

Stepped approach

  • Step 1: Lifestyle modifications (DASH diet, Na⁺ restriction <2.3 g/day, weight loss, exercise, limit alcohol)
  • Step 2: Start monotherapy with one first-line agent (based on comorbidities)
  • Step 3: If not at goal → increase dose or add a second agent from a different class
  • Step 4: If still not controlled → triple therapy (commonly: ACE inhibitor/ARB + CCB + thiazide diuretic)
  • Resistant hypertension (uncontrolled on 3 drugs including diuretic) → add spironolactone as fourth agent

Comorbidity-based preferred choices

Comorbidity Preferred drug(s)
Diabetes mellitus ACE inhibitor or ARB (renoprotective)
Heart failure (HFrEF) ACE inhibitor/ARB + β-blocker + diuretic + spironolactone
Post-MI β-blocker + ACE inhibitor
CKD with proteinuria ACE inhibitor or ARB
Elderly / Isolated systolic HTN Thiazide or CCB (DHP)
Black patients Thiazide or CCB (less responsive to ACE inhibitors/ARBs as monotherapy)
Pregnancy Methyldopa (safest), labetalol, nifedipine — avoid ACE/ARB
BPH α₁-blocker (prazosin, doxazosin) — dual benefit
Migraine β-blocker (propranolol) or CCB
Asthma/COPD Avoid β-blockers; use CCB, ACE inhibitor, or ARB
Pheochromocytoma α-blocker first (phenoxybenzamine), then β-blocker

Oral exam pearls

  • The four first-line classes: A-B-C-D = ACE/ARB, Beta-blocker (now second-line in uncomplicated HTN), CCB, Diuretic
  • Never combine ACE inhibitor + ARB — dual RAAS blockade increases harm
  • β-blockers are no longer first-line for uncomplicated hypertension (but remain first-line for HF, post-MI, rate control)
  • Pheochromocytoma: always block α BEFORE β (to avoid hypertensive crisis from unopposed α stimulation)
Say it aloud — 30–60 sec

Lifestyle first: Salt restriction, DASH diet, weight loss, exercise and limiting alcohol. First-line classes: Thiazides, ACE inhibitors, ARBs and calcium channel blockers. Choice by comorbidity: ACE inhibitor/ARB for diabetes and CKD; thiazide or CCB for the elderly and Black patients; beta-blocker plus ACE inhibitor after MI; methyldopa or labetalol in pregnancy. Stepwise: Start monotherapy, then combine classes — a common triple is ACE/ARB + CCB + thiazide, adding spironolactone for resistant hypertension. Never combine ACE inhibitor with ARB, and in phaeochromocytoma always block alpha before beta.

I.14Thiazide Diuretics

Definition/Intro

Thiazide diuretics (e.g., hydrochlorothiazide, chlorthalidone, indapamide) act on the distal convoluted tubule and are first-line for hypertension.

Mechanism of action

  • Inhibit the Na⁺/Cl⁻ cotransporter (NCC) in the early distal convoluted tubule (DCT)
  • Increased Na⁺ delivery to collecting duct → enhances Na⁺/K⁺ exchange → K⁺ wasting
  • Also decrease Ca²⁺ excretion (increase Ca²⁺ reabsorption) — opposite of loop diuretics
  • Initially reduce BP by decreasing blood volume; chronically, by decreasing peripheral vascular resistance (PVR)

Pharmacological effects

  • Moderate diuresis (less potent than loop diuretics)
  • Decreased blood pressure
  • Hypocalciuria (retain Ca²⁺) — useful in Ca²⁺ stone formers and osteoporosis
  • Ineffective when GFR <30 mL/min (except metolazone, which works even at low GFR)

Therapeutic uses

  • Hypertension — first-line; preferred in elderly, Black patients, isolated systolic HTN
  • Heart failure (mild, adjunct)
  • Nephrogenic diabetes insipidus (paradoxical — reduces urine volume by increasing proximal reabsorption)
  • Calcium-containing kidney stone prevention (decreases urinary Ca²⁺)
  • Edema (mild)
  • Osteoporosis (adjunctive — Ca²⁺ retention)

Adverse effects — mnemonic: “HyperGLUC”

  • Hypokalemia (most important — can potentiate digoxin toxicity)
  • Hyponatremia (most common electrolyte abnormality causing hospitalization)
  • Hypercalcemia (increased Ca²⁺ reabsorption)
  • Hyperuricemia → gout (compete with uric acid for secretion in proximal tubule)
  • Hyperglycemia (impair insulin release due to hypokalemia; direct pancreatic effect)
  • Hyperlipidemia (increased LDL, triglycerides)
  • Metabolic alkalosis (due to volume contraction and K⁺ loss)
  • Sulfonamide allergy cross-reactivity (thiazides are sulfonamide derivatives)

Oral exam pearls

  • Thiazides save calcium (hyper-Ca²⁺); loops lose calcium (hypo-Ca²⁺) — opposite effects
  • Hypokalemia with thiazides is clinically critical if the patient is on digoxin
  • Use in nephrogenic DI is a classic board question (paradoxical antidiuretic effect)
  • Chlorthalidone has a longer half-life and stronger evidence base than HCTZ
Say it aloud — 30–60 sec

Mechanism: Inhibit the Na/Cl cotransporter in the early distal convoluted tubule, and they increase calcium reabsorption. Pharmacological effects: Moderate diuresis, lower blood pressure (chronically by reducing peripheral resistance), and reduce urinary calcium. Therapeutic uses: First-line hypertension (good in elderly and Black patients), calcium kidney stones, and paradoxically nephrogenic diabetes insipidus. Adverse effects: The metabolic set — hyponatraemia, hypokalaemia, hypercalcaemia, hyperuricaemia (gout), hyperglycaemia, hyperlipidaemia — plus metabolic alkalosis; they’re sulfonamides, so cross-allergy is possible.

I.15Loop Diuretics (Bumetanide, Furosemide, Torsemide, Ethacrynic Acid)

Definition/Intro

Loop diuretics are the most potent (high-ceiling) diuretics; they act on the thick ascending limb of the loop of Henle. Prototypes: furosemide, bumetanide, torsemide, ethacrynic acid.

Mechanism of action

  • Inhibit the Na⁺/K⁺/2Cl⁻ cotransporter (NKCC2) in the thick ascending limb (TAL) of the loop of Henle
  • Block reabsorption of ~25% of filtered Na⁺ → massive natriuresis and diuresis
  • Abolish the corticomedullary osmotic gradient → impair concentrating ability
  • Increase Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺ excretion (by eliminating the lumen-positive potential that drives paracellular cation reabsorption)
  • Also increase renal blood flow (prostaglandin-mediated venodilation — furosemide)

Pharmacological effects

  • Potent diuresis — can remove liters of fluid
  • Decreased preload (venodilation occurs even before diuresis with IV furosemide)
  • Increased excretion of Na⁺, K⁺, Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺, Cl⁻, H⁺
  • Effective even at low GFR (unlike thiazides)

Therapeutic uses

  • Acute pulmonary edema (IV furosemide — rapid preload reduction)
  • Congestive heart failure with volume overload
  • Edema (hepatic cirrhosis, nephrotic syndrome, renal failure)
  • Acute renal failure (maintain urine output)
  • Hypercalcemia (emergency treatment — saline + furosemide to promote calciuresis)
  • Hyperkalemia (promote K⁺ excretion)
  • Cerebral edema (adjunct)

Adverse effects

  • Hypokalemia → can cause arrhythmias, potentiate digoxin toxicity
  • Metabolic alkalosis (contraction alkalosis; H⁺/K⁺ loss)
  • Hypocalcemia, hypomagnesemia (opposite of thiazides — loops LOSE calcium)
  • Ototoxicity (especially with rapid IV administration; ethacrynic acid has highest risk — avoid with aminoglycosides)
  • Hyperuricemia → gout
  • Hyperglycemia (less than thiazides)
  • Dehydration, hypovolemia, hypotension
  • Hyponatremia
  • Sulfonamide allergy — all loop diuretics are sulfonamide derivatives EXCEPT ethacrynic acid (use in sulfa-allergic patients)
  • Interstitial nephritis (rare)

Oral exam pearls

  • Loops lose calcium; thiazides save calcium — a top oral-exam comparison
  • Ethacrynic acid = only non-sulfonamide loop diuretic (safe in sulfa allergy); also highest ototoxicity
  • Furosemide IV in acute pulmonary edema provides venodilation within minutes, before diuresis begins
  • Ototoxicity risk is additive with aminoglycosides — avoid combination if possible
Say it aloud — 30–60 sec

Mechanism: Inhibit the Na/K/2Cl cotransporter in the thick ascending limb — the most potent diuretics — and they increase calcium and magnesium loss. Pharmacological effects: Massive natriuresis and diuresis, effective even at low GFR, plus venodilation that reduces preload. Therapeutic uses: Acute pulmonary oedema, heart failure with fluid overload, oedema, and hypercalcaemia. Adverse effects: Hypokalaemia, metabolic alkalosis, hypocalcaemia and hypomagnesaemia, ototoxicity, hyperuricaemia and dehydration. Ethacrynic acid is the only non-sulfonamide loop (use in sulfa allergy) but has the highest ototoxicity.

I.16Spironolactone and Eplerenone

Definition/Intro

Spironolactone and eplerenone are potassium-sparing diuretics that act as aldosterone receptor antagonists in the collecting duct.

Mechanism of action

  • Competitively block mineralocorticoid (aldosterone) receptors in the principal cells of the late DCT and cortical collecting duct
  • Prevent aldosterone-induced expression of ENaC (epithelial Na⁺ channels) and Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase
  • Result: decreased Na⁺ reabsorption and decreased K⁺ secretion → mild diuresis with K⁺ retention
  • Spironolactone also binds androgen and progesterone receptors (nonselective) → endocrine side effects
  • Eplerenone is selective for the mineralocorticoid receptor → fewer endocrine side effects

Pharmacological effects

  • Mild diuresis (weak when used alone — usually combined with thiazides or loops)
  • Potassium sparing (reduced K⁺ and H⁺ excretion)
  • Decreased blood pressure (modest)
  • Anti-fibrotic and anti-remodeling effects in the heart (independent of diuretic action)

Therapeutic uses

  • Heart failure (HFrEF) — proven mortality reduction (RALES trial for spironolactone; EPHESUS for eplerenone post-MI)
  • Resistant hypertension — excellent fourth-line agent
  • Primary hyperaldosteronism (Conn syndrome) — spironolactone is drug of choice for medical management
  • Hepatic cirrhosis with ascites (aldosterone is elevated; spironolactone is diuretic of choice)
  • Hypokalemia prevention when combined with K⁺-wasting diuretics
  • Hirsutism and acne in females (spironolactone — anti-androgen effect, off-label)
  • Polycystic ovary syndrome (spironolactone — anti-androgen)

Adverse effects

  • Hyperkalemia (most dangerous — especially when combined with ACE inhibitors, ARBs, K⁺ supplements, or in renal insufficiency)
  • Spironolactone-specific (antiandrogenic/progesterone effects):
  • Gynecomastia and breast tenderness in males (most common reason to switch to eplerenone)
  • Menstrual irregularities in females
  • Sexual dysfunction, decreased libido
  • Eplerenone: hyperkalemia without the endocrine side effects (selective)
  • Metabolic acidosis (hyperkalemic, type 4 RTA)
  • GI upset

Oral exam pearls

  • Spironolactone has mortality benefit in HF — not just a diuretic, it is disease-modifying
  • Gynecomastia in a male on spironolactone → switch to eplerenone
  • Used in Conn syndrome (primary hyperaldosteronism) — blocks excess aldosterone
  • Most dangerous complication: life-threatening hyperkalemia, especially with concurrent RAAS blockade
Say it aloud — 30–60 sec

Mechanism: Competitively block the aldosterone (mineralocorticoid) receptor in the collecting duct, reducing sodium reabsorption while sparing potassium. Pharmacological effects: Mild diuresis with potassium retention plus anti-fibrotic cardiac effects. Therapeutic uses: Heart failure (reduce mortality — RALES/EPHESUS), primary hyperaldosteronism (Conn’s), cirrhosis with ascites, and resistant hypertension as a fourth agent. Adverse effects: Hyperkalaemia (dangerous with ACE inhibitors/ARBs); spironolactone also causes gynaecomastia and menstrual changes (hits androgen/progesterone receptors) — switch to the selective eplerenone.

I.17Acetazolamide

Definition/Intro

Acetazolamide is a carbonic anhydrase inhibitor that acts primarily in the proximal convoluted tubule (PCT); it is a weak diuretic.

Mechanism of action

  • Inhibits carbonic anhydrase (CA) in the luminal membrane and cytoplasm of proximal tubule cells
  • CA normally catalyzes: CO₂ + H₂O ↔ H₂CO₃ ↔ H⁺ + HCO₃⁻
  • Inhibition → decreased H⁺ secretion into lumen → decreased Na⁺/H⁺ exchange → decreased NaHCO₃ reabsorption
  • Result: bicarbonate-rich, alkaline urine (self-limiting diuresis as body becomes acidotic)
  • Also inhibits CA in the ciliary body → decreased aqueous humor production → reduced IOP

Pharmacokinetics

  • Well absorbed orally
  • Concentrated in tissues rich in carbonic anhydrase (kidney, eye, CNS, RBCs)
  • Excreted renally (unchanged drug)
  • Self-limiting effect: as plasma HCO₃⁻ falls and metabolic acidosis develops, less HCO₃⁻ is filtered → drug becomes ineffective

Pharmacological effects

  • Mild diuresis (NaHCO₃ excretion; self-limiting)
  • Metabolic acidosis (loss of bicarbonate)
  • Alkaline urine (HCO₃⁻ wasting)
  • Decreased intraocular pressure
  • Decreased CSF production (relevant in pseudotumor cerebri)

Therapeutic uses

  • Glaucoma (open-angle) — reduces aqueous humor formation
  • Altitude sickness (acute mountain sickness) — prophylaxis and treatment; metabolic acidosis stimulates respiratory compensation (hyperventilation) and offsets respiratory alkalosis at altitude
  • Metabolic alkalosis correction (acetazolamide promotes HCO₃⁻ excretion)
  • Pseudotumor cerebri (idiopathic intracranial hypertension) — decreases CSF production
  • Epilepsy (adjunct — rarely used now)
  • Urinary alkalinization (e.g., to promote excretion of acidic drugs like aspirin in overdose)

Adverse effects

  • Metabolic acidosis (hyperchloremic, non-anion-gap — due to HCO₃⁻ wasting)
  • Hypokalemia (increased Na⁺ delivery to collecting duct → enhanced K⁺ secretion)
  • Renal stones (calcium phosphate — alkaline urine + decreased citrate excretion)
  • Paresthesias (tingling of extremities — very common)
  • Drowsiness, fatigue
  • Sulfonamide allergy cross-reactivity
  • Ammonia toxicity in hepatic cirrhosis (decreased renal NH₄⁺ excretion → contraindicated in hepatic failure)
  • Self-limiting diuretic efficacy (metabolic acidosis reduces drug effect)

Oral exam pearls

  • Think of acetazolamide for two classic scenarios: altitude sickness and glaucoma
  • It causes metabolic acidosis (non-anion-gap) — opposite of loop/thiazide-induced alkalosis
  • Self-limiting effect: becomes less effective as metabolic acidosis develops
  • Contraindicated in hepatic cirrhosis (ammonia accumulation → encephalopathy)
Say it aloud — 30–60 sec

Mechanism: Inhibits carbonic anhydrase in the proximal tubule, blocking bicarbonate reabsorption, so alkaline bicarbonate-rich urine is produced. Pharmacokinetics: Oral, renally excreted; effect is self-limiting because as metabolic acidosis develops less bicarbonate is filtered. Pharmacological effects: Mild diuresis, metabolic acidosis, lower intraocular pressure and reduced CSF production. Therapeutic uses: Open-angle glaucoma, altitude sickness (acidosis drives compensatory hyperventilation), metabolic alkalosis and pseudotumor cerebri. Adverse effects: Metabolic acidosis, hypokalaemia, calcium-phosphate kidney stones and paraesthesia; avoid in cirrhosis (encephalopathy risk).

I.18Centrally Acting Adrenergic Drugs: Clonidine and α-Methyldopa

Definition/Intro

Clonidine and α-methyldopa reduce sympathetic outflow from the CNS to lower blood pressure; they are second-line antihypertensives with specific niche uses.

Mechanism of action

  • Clonidine: activates α₂-adrenergic receptors (and imidazoline receptors) in the brainstem vasomotor center (nucleus tractus solitarius) → decreased sympathetic outflow → reduced heart rate, cardiac output, and peripheral vascular resistance
  • α-Methyldopa: prodrug → converted in adrenergic neurons to α-methyl-norepinephrine, which is a potent α₂-agonist in the brainstem → same central sympatholytic effect as clonidine

Therapeutic uses

  • Clonidine:
  • Hypertension (second-line; especially with renal disease)
  • Opioid withdrawal, alcohol withdrawal (reduces sympathetic hyperactivity)
  • ADHD (second-line, especially with tics or Tourette)
  • Menopausal flushing (off-label)
  • Diagnosis of pheochromocytoma (clonidine suppression test)
  • α-Methyldopa:
  • Hypertension in pregnancy — drug of choice (safest antihypertensive with longest safety record in pregnancy)
  • Chronic hypertension (rarely used outside pregnancy today)

Adverse effects

  • Both: sedation/drowsiness, dry mouth, bradycardia, orthostatic hypotension, sexual dysfunction
  • Clonidine:
  • Rebound hypertension on abrupt withdrawal (severe — due to sudden surge of catecholamines; must taper)
  • Constipation
  • α-Methyldopa:
  • Hemolytic anemia (Coombs-positive — autoimmune) — most important unique toxicity
  • Hepatotoxicity
  • Drug-induced lupus-like syndrome
  • Sedation (prominent)
  • Positive direct Coombs test in up to 20% of patients (hemolytic anemia in ~5%)

Oral exam pearls

  • Methyldopa = pregnancy drug of choice — this is the number-one association to know
  • Clonidine = rebound hypertension on abrupt withdrawal — always taper
  • Methyldopa = Coombs-positive hemolytic anemia — classic board question
  • Both reduce central sympathetic tone via α₂ agonism — different mechanisms to get there
Say it aloud — 30–60 sec

Mechanism: Both stimulate central alpha-2 receptors in the brainstem, reducing sympathetic outflow and lowering blood pressure; methyldopa is a prodrug converted to alpha-methyl-noradrenaline. Therapeutic uses: Methyldopa is the antihypertensive of choice in pregnancy; clonidine is used in resistant hypertension, opioid and alcohol withdrawal, and ADHD. Adverse effects: Both cause sedation, dry mouth, bradycardia and orthostatic hypotension. Methyldopa specifically causes Coombs-positive haemolytic anaemia and hepatotoxicity; clonidine causes severe rebound hypertension if stopped abruptly — always taper.

I.19Vasodilators: Hydralazine and Minoxidil

Definition/Intro

Hydralazine and minoxidil are direct-acting arteriolar vasodilators used in hypertension; they preferentially dilate arterioles (reduce afterload) with minimal venodilation.

Mechanism of action

  • Hydralazine:
  • Directly relaxes arteriolar smooth muscle (mechanism not fully understood; involves increased NO, cGMP, and/or interference with IP₃-mediated Ca²⁺ release from sarcoplasmic reticulum)
  • Selective for arterioles → reduces afterload (SVR); minimal venodilation
  • Minoxidil:
  • Opens K⁺_ATP channels in arteriolar smooth muscle → K⁺ efflux → hyperpolarization → relaxation → potent arteriolar vasodilation
  • Among the most powerful oral vasodilators available

Therapeutic uses

  • Hydralazine:
  • Hypertension (usually combined with β-blocker + diuretic to counteract reflex responses)
  • Heart failure — hydralazine + isosorbide dinitrate (HYD-ISDN) combination: proven mortality benefit in Black patients with HFrEF (A-HeFT trial); also used when ACE inhibitor/ARB is contraindicated
  • Hypertensive emergency in pregnancy (IV hydralazine)
  • Minoxidil:
  • Severe/refractory hypertension (reserved for resistant cases not responding to standard therapy)
  • Topical for androgenic alopecia (hair growth — a therapeutic use derived from its side effect)

Adverse effects

  • Both cause reflex tachycardia and fluid retention (due to arteriolar dilation → baroreceptor activation → sympathetic surge → ↑HR, ↑renin, ↑Na⁺/H₂O retention) — must combine with β-blocker (for tachycardia) and diuretic (for fluid retention) — “triple therapy”
  • Hydralazine:
  • Drug-induced lupus (SLE-like syndrome) — most important toxicity; dose-dependent (>200 mg/day); more common in slow acetylators; presents with arthralgias, myalgias, fever, rash; anti-histone antibodies positive; usually spares kidneys; reversible on discontinuation
  • Headache, flushing, palpitations
  • Minoxidil:
  • Hypertrichosis (excessive hair growth — entire body; hence topical use for baldness)
  • Pericardial effusion / pericarditis (can be serious)
  • Severe fluid retention, edema

Oral exam pearls

  • Hydralazine = drug-induced lupus (anti-histone antibodies, slow acetylators) — top association
  • Minoxidil = hypertrichosis (and topical for hair loss) + pericardial effusion
  • Both require “triple therapy” (vasodilator + β-blocker + diuretic) to counteract reflex tachycardia and fluid retention

II.1Levodopa and Carbidopa

  • Mechanism of action
  • Levodopa is the metabolic precursor of dopamine; it crosses the BBB via large neutral amino acid transporter and is converted to dopamine by DOPA decarboxylase in the CNS, replenishing depleted striatal dopamine.

  • Carbidopa is a peripheral DOPA decarboxylase inhibitor — it does NOT cross the BBB, so it blocks peripheral conversion of levodopa to dopamine, increasing CNS bioavailability and reducing peripheral side effects.

  • Pharmacokinetics

  • Levodopa is rapidly absorbed from the small intestine; absorption is decreased by high-protein meals (competes with large neutral amino acids for transport).
  • Short half-life (~1–2 h); given with carbidopa to reduce the required dose of levodopa by ~4-fold.

  • Extensive first-pass metabolism if given alone (>95% peripherally decarboxylated).

  • Therapeutic uses

  • First-line for motor symptoms of Parkinson disease (bradykinesia, rigidity respond best; tremor less so).

  • Most effective anti-Parkinson drug — all patients eventually require it.

  • Adverse effects

  • Peripheral (reduced by carbidopa): nausea, vomiting, orthostatic hypotension, cardiac arrhythmias.

  • Central: dyskinesias (choreiform movements — most common dose-limiting effect), “on–off” phenomenon, wearing-off effect, behavioral changes (hallucinations, confusion, psychosis), vivid dreams.

  • Interactions / Contraindications

  • Non-selective MAO inhibitors → hypertensive crisis (MAO-B selective inhibitors are safe).
  • Antipsychotics (D2 blockers) antagonize its effect; avoid typical antipsychotics — use quetiapine or clozapine if psychosis occurs.

  • Pyridoxine (vitamin B6) enhances peripheral decarboxylation and reduces efficacy unless carbidopa is co-administered.

  • Oral exam pearls

  • “Carbidopa cannot cross the BBB” — this is WHY it selectively blocks peripheral decarboxylation.
  • High-protein meals reduce levodopa absorption — advise patients to distribute protein intake.
  • Dyskinesias appear with chronic use; “on–off” fluctuations worsen over years.
  • Levodopa treats symptoms, not disease progression.
Say it aloud — 30–60 sec

Mechanism: Levodopa crosses the blood-brain barrier and is converted by DOPA decarboxylase into dopamine, replacing what’s lost in Parkinson’s; carbidopa inhibits this conversion peripherally (it can’t cross the BBB), so more levodopa reaches the brain and peripheral side effects fall. Pharmacokinetics: Short half-life; high-protein meals reduce absorption; carbidopa cuts the required dose about four-fold. Therapeutic uses: The most effective drug for Parkinson’s, best for bradykinesia and rigidity. Adverse effects: Nausea, orthostatic hypotension, dyskinesias, on-off fluctuations and hallucinations. Interactions: Non-selective MAO inhibitors risk hypertensive crisis; antipsychotics and pyridoxine (without carbidopa) reduce its effect.

II.2Parkinson — MAO-B Inhibitors (Selegiline, Rasagiline)

  • Mechanism of action
  • Selectively and irreversibly inhibit MAO-B, the predominant MAO isoform in the striatum, thereby preventing the breakdown of dopamine and increasing its availability in the synapse.

  • At therapeutic doses, they do NOT inhibit MAO-A (which metabolizes norepinephrine, serotonin, and tyramine in the gut), so the “cheese reaction” risk is minimal.

  • Pharmacokinetics

  • Selegiline: metabolized to amphetamine and methamphetamine derivatives — can cause insomnia; available as oral and transdermal patch.
  • Rasagiline: more potent, no amphetamine metabolites — better tolerated; once-daily dosing.

  • Both are irreversible inhibitors — effect lasts until new enzyme is synthesized.

  • Indication

  • Early or mild Parkinson disease as monotherapy.

  • Adjunct to levodopa/carbidopa to reduce “wearing-off” and extend levodopa’s effect in advanced disease.

  • Adverse effects

  • Selegiline: insomnia (amphetamine metabolites), nausea, dizziness, headache.
  • At high doses, selectivity for MAO-B is lost → risk of hypertensive crisis with tyramine-rich foods.

  • Serotonin syndrome risk when combined with meperidine, SSRIs, or SNRIs.

  • Oral exam pearls

  • “Selective for MAO-B at therapeutic doses” — selectivity is lost at high doses.
  • Rasagiline is preferred over selegiline because it lacks amphetamine metabolites.
  • Never combine with meperidine — serotonin syndrome risk.
Say it aloud — 30–60 sec

Mechanism: Selectively and irreversibly inhibit MAO-B, preventing dopamine breakdown in the striatum. Pharmacokinetics: Selegiline forms amphetamine metabolites (causing insomnia); rasagiline has none and is better tolerated. Indication: Monotherapy in early Parkinson’s or add-on to levodopa to reduce wearing-off. Adverse effects: Insomnia and headache; selectivity is lost at high doses (tyramine reaction), and they must not be combined with meperidine or SSRIs (serotonin syndrome).

II.3Parkinson — COMT Inhibitors (Entacapone, Tolcapone)

  • Mechanism of action
  • Inhibit catechol-O-methyltransferase (COMT), which normally degrades levodopa to 3-O-methyldopa in the periphery.
  • By blocking this pathway, they increase the plasma half-life and bioavailability of levodopa, allowing more to reach the CNS.

  • Always used as adjuncts with levodopa/carbidopa — they have no anti-parkinsonian effect alone.

  • Pharmacokinetics

  • Entacapone: acts only peripherally; short-acting — given with each dose of levodopa. Available as a triple combination (Stalevo = levodopa + carbidopa + entacapone).

  • Tolcapone: acts both peripherally and centrally (crosses BBB); longer duration of action but associated with hepatotoxicity.

  • Indication

  • Adjunct to levodopa/carbidopa in patients experiencing “wearing-off” fluctuations.

  • Adverse effects

  • Diarrhea, nausea, dyskinesias, urine discoloration (orange-brown).
  • Enhanced dopaminergic side effects (because they increase levodopa levels — may need to reduce levodopa dose).

  • Tolcapone: fatal hepatotoxicity — requires liver function monitoring; reserved for patients not responding to entacapone.

  • Oral exam pearls

  • “No standalone effect” — COMT inhibitors are useless without levodopa.
  • Entacapone is first-line among the two because it is safer (peripheral only, no hepatotoxicity).
  • Tolcapone crosses the BBB but carries hepatotoxicity risk — needs LFT monitoring.
  • Orange urine is a harmless but classic board-tested finding.
Say it aloud — 30–60 sec

Mechanism: Inhibit COMT, which breaks levodopa down to 3-O-methyldopa, so levodopa’s half-life and brain delivery increase — no effect alone. Pharmacokinetics: Entacapone acts only peripherally and is short-acting; tolcapone also acts centrally and lasts longer. Indication: Add-on to levodopa/carbidopa for wearing-off. Adverse effects: Diarrhoea, nausea, more dyskinesia and harmless orange urine; tolcapone can cause fatal hepatotoxicity and needs liver monitoring.

II.4Parkinson — Dopamine Agonists

  • Mechanism of action
  • Directly stimulate dopamine receptors (D2 primarily, also D3) in the striatum, bypassing the need for enzymatic conversion from levodopa.

  • Unlike levodopa, their action does not depend on the degenerating dopaminergic neurons.

  • Classification / Key drugs

  • Ergot derivatives: bromocriptine (rarely used now — fibrotic complications).

  • Non-ergot derivatives (preferred): pramipexole, ropinirole (oral); rotigotine (transdermal patch); apomorphine (subcutaneous injection for acute “off” episodes).

  • Pharmacokinetics

  • Pramipexole: renally excreted (dose-adjust in renal impairment); long half-life.
  • Ropinirole: hepatically metabolized via CYP1A2.
  • Rotigotine: transdermal patch provides continuous dopaminergic stimulation.

  • Apomorphine: rapid onset (10–20 min SC); NOT orally bioavailable; highly emetogenic — requires pretreatment with trimethobenzamide.

  • Indication

  • Monotherapy in early Parkinson disease (delay levodopa initiation).
  • Adjunct to levodopa in advanced disease to reduce “off” time.

  • Apomorphine: rescue therapy for acute, intermittent “off” episodes.

  • Adverse effects

  • Nausea, somnolence, orthostatic hypotension, hallucinations/confusion (worse in elderly).
  • Impulse control disorders (pathological gambling, hypersexuality, compulsive shopping) — high-yield.
  • Sleep attacks (sudden onset of sleep — warn patients about driving).

  • Ergot derivatives: pulmonary and retroperitoneal fibrosis, valvular heart disease.

  • Oral exam pearls

  • Non-ergot agonists are preferred — ergot derivatives cause fibrosis.
  • Impulse control disorders are the classic board buzzword for dopamine agonists.
  • Apomorphine is given SC, not orally; pretreat with antiemetic (NOT ondansetron — profound hypotension).
  • Less dyskinesia than levodopa but also less efficacious for motor symptoms.
Say it aloud — 30–60 sec

Mechanism: Directly stimulate dopamine D2 receptors, independent of the degenerating neurons; non-ergot agents (pramipexole, ropinirole, rotigotine) are preferred over ergot ones. Pharmacokinetics: Pramipexole is renally cleared, ropinirole is hepatic; apomorphine is a subcutaneous rescue for acute ‘off’ episodes. Indication: Monotherapy in early Parkinson’s or add-on to reduce ‘off’ time. Adverse effects: Nausea, orthostatic hypotension, hallucinations, sudden sleep attacks, and impulse-control disorders (gambling, hypersexuality); ergot types cause fibrosis.

II.5Treatment of Seizures: Classification and Mechanisms

  • Classification of antiseizure drugs by mechanism 1. Sodium channel blockers (prolong inactivated state): phenytoin, carbamazepine, lamotrigine, oxcarbazepine, lacosamide. 2. Calcium current inhibitors (T-type Ca2+ channels): ethosuximide (absence seizures), valproic acid (partial mechanism). 3. GABA enhancement: benzodiazepines and barbiturates (enhance GABA_A receptor), tiagabine (blocks GABA reuptake), vigabatrin (irreversibly inhibits GABA transaminase), valproic acid (increases GABA levels). 4. Glutamate / excitatory reduction: felbamate (blocks NMDA), topiramate (blocks AMPA/kainate), perampanel (AMPA antagonist). 5. SV2A binding: levetiracetam (binds synaptic vesicle protein SV2A, modulating neurotransmitter release). 6. Multiple mechanisms: valproic acid (Na+ channel block + T-type Ca2+ block + increased GABA), topiramate (Na+ channel + GABA enhancement + glutamate block).

  • Classification by seizure type

  • Focal (partial) seizures: carbamazepine, phenytoin, lamotrigine, levetiracetam, oxcarbazepine, valproic acid.
  • Generalized tonic-clonic: valproic acid, lamotrigine, levetiracetam, phenytoin, carbamazepine.
  • Absence seizures: ethosuximide (drug of choice), valproic acid (alternative or if coexisting generalized tonic-clonic seizures).
  • Myoclonic: valproic acid (drug of choice), levetiracetam.

  • Status epilepticus: IV benzodiazepines (lorazepam first-line) → IV fosphenytoin/phenytoin → IV phenobarbital/valproic acid.

  • Oral exam pearls

  • Valproic acid is the broadest-spectrum antiseizure drug — works on almost all seizure types.
  • Ethosuximide is ONLY for absence seizures — no effect on generalized tonic-clonic.
  • Carbamazepine can worsen absence and myoclonic seizures.
  • Status epilepticus first-line = IV lorazepam.
Say it aloud — 30–60 sec

Mechanisms: Antiseizure drugs work four ways — block sodium channels (phenytoin, carbamazepine, lamotrigine); block T-type calcium channels (ethosuximide); enhance GABA (benzodiazepines, barbiturates, vigabatrin); or reduce glutamate (perampanel, topiramate). Levetiracetam binds SV2A; valproate uses several mechanisms. By seizure type: Focal → carbamazepine/lamotrigine; generalised tonic-clonic → valproate/lamotrigine; absence → ethosuximide; myoclonic → valproate; status epilepticus → IV lorazepam first, then fosphenytoin. Valproate is the broadest-spectrum agent.

II.6Carbamazepine

  • Mechanism of action
  • Blocks voltage-gated sodium channels by binding preferentially to the inactivated state, prolonging inactivation and reducing high-frequency repetitive neuronal firing.

  • Does not affect normal low-frequency firing — selective for epileptic foci.

  • Pharmacokinetics

  • Absorbed slowly and erratically from the GI tract.
  • Highly protein-bound (~75%).
  • Potent inducer of CYP3A4 (and other CYP enzymes) — induces its OWN metabolism (autoinduction), so the half-life decreases from ~36 h initially to ~12–17 h with chronic use.
  • Must monitor plasma levels; dose adjustments needed after 2–4 weeks due to autoinduction.

  • Active metabolite: carbamazepine-10,11-epoxide.

  • Adverse effects

  • CNS: diplopia, blurred vision, ataxia, sedation, dizziness (dose-related).
  • Hematologic: aplastic anemia, agranulocytosis (rare but serious — monitor CBC).
  • Dermatologic: Stevens-Johnson syndrome / toxic epidermal necrolysis (associated with HLA-B*1502 allele — screen in Asian patients before prescribing).
  • Metabolic: hyponatremia (SIADH-like effect).
  • Hepatic: hepatotoxicity.
  • Drug interactions: as a CYP inducer, it reduces levels of oral contraceptives, warfarin, valproic acid, and many other drugs.

  • Contraindicated in absence seizures (can worsen them).

  • Oral exam pearls

  • “Autoinduction” — carbamazepine induces its own metabolism; half-life drops with chronic use.
  • Screen for HLA-B*1502 before starting in patients of Asian descent — SJS/TEN risk.
  • Causes SIADH/hyponatremia — unique among antiseizure drugs.
  • A potent CYP inducer — always check drug interactions.
Say it aloud — 30–60 sec

Mechanism: Blocks voltage-gated sodium channels in the inactivated state, reducing high-frequency firing. Pharmacokinetics: Slow, erratic absorption; a potent CYP3A4 inducer that induces its own metabolism (autoinduction), so half-life falls over weeks and it lowers many drug levels. Adverse effects: Diplopia, ataxia and sedation; hyponatraemia (SIADH-like); aplastic anaemia and agranulocytosis (monitor blood counts); and Stevens-Johnson syndrome linked to HLA-B*1502 — screen Asian patients. It can worsen absence seizures.

II.7Valproic Acid

  • Mechanism of action
  • Multiple mechanisms: blocks voltage-gated sodium channels (inactivated state), blocks T-type calcium channels, and increases brain GABA levels by inhibiting GABA transaminase and enhancing GABA synthesis.

  • This multi-mechanism profile gives it the broadest spectrum of any antiseizure drug.

  • Pharmacokinetics

  • Well absorbed orally; highly protein-bound (~90%).
  • Inhibits CYP enzymes (CYP2C9 and others) — raises levels of co-administered drugs (opposite of carbamazepine).
  • Metabolized by the liver via glucuronidation and beta-oxidation.
  • Half-life ~9–16 hours.

  • Displaces other drugs from plasma proteins (e.g., displaces phenytoin → increased free phenytoin).

  • Adverse effects

  • GI: nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain (most common; take with food).
  • Hepatic: fatal hepatotoxicity (greatest risk in children <2 years on polytherapy — boxed warning).
  • Hematologic: thrombocytopenia, inhibition of platelet aggregation.
  • Teratogenicity: neural tube defects (spina bifida) — absolutely contraindicated in pregnancy; supplement with folate if used in women of childbearing age.
  • Metabolic: weight gain, alopecia (hair loss with curly regrowth).
  • Pancreatitis (rare but life-threatening).

  • Tremor.

  • Oral exam pearls

  • Broadest-spectrum antiseizure drug — works for absence, tonic-clonic, myoclonic, and focal seizures.
  • “Neural tube defects” — the #1 teratogenic antiseizure drug; avoid in pregnancy.
  • Enzyme inhibitor (unlike carbamazepine which is an inducer).
  • Fatal hepatotoxicity in children <2 years — boxed warning.
Say it aloud — 30–60 sec

Pharmacokinetics: Well absorbed, ~90% protein-bound, and a CYP inhibitor that raises other drug levels and displaces phenytoin. Adverse effects: Fatal hepatotoxicity (highest risk in children under 2 on polytherapy — boxed warning), teratogenic neural tube defects (avoid in pregnancy), thrombocytopenia, weight gain, hair loss, tremor and pancreatitis.

II.8Phenytoin

  • Mechanism of action

  • Blocks voltage-gated sodium channels by binding to the inactivated state, prolonging channel inactivation and selectively reducing sustained high-frequency repetitive firing without affecting normal neuronal activity.

  • Pharmacokinetics

  • Absorbed slowly; bioavailability varies between formulations.
  • Highly protein-bound (~90%) — displaced by other drugs (e.g., valproic acid), leading to toxicity at “therapeutic” total levels.
  • Zero-order (saturation) kinetics at therapeutic doses — small dose increases cause disproportionately large increases in plasma concentration; requires careful dose titration and monitoring.
  • Metabolized by CYP2C9 and CYP2C19; potent CYP3A4 inducer (reduces levels of oral contraceptives, warfarin, etc.).

  • IV formulation: use fosphenytoin (prodrug) to avoid cardiovascular toxicity of phenytoin’s propylene glycol vehicle.

  • Adverse effects

  • CNS (dose-related): nystagmus (earliest sign of toxicity), diplopia, ataxia, sedation, cognitive impairment.
  • Gingival hyperplasia — classic board finding; requires dental hygiene.
  • Hirsutism, coarsening of facial features.
  • Teratogenicity: fetal hydantoin syndrome (cleft palate, cardiac defects, digital hypoplasia).
  • Dermatologic: SJS/TEN (rare), morbilliform rash.
  • Megaloblastic anemia (interferes with folate metabolism).
  • Osteomalacia (induces CYP metabolism of vitamin D).
  • Peripheral neuropathy with chronic use.

  • IV phenytoin: purple glove syndrome, cardiac arrhythmias, hypotension.

  • Oral exam pearls

  • “Zero-order kinetics” — the single most tested PK concept for phenytoin; tiny dose changes can cause toxicity.
  • Nystagmus is the earliest sign of toxicity.
  • Gingival hyperplasia + hirsutism = phenytoin (classic exam combo).
  • Highly protein-bound + CYP inducer = interaction-heavy drug.
Say it aloud — 30–60 sec

Mechanism: Blocks sodium channels in the inactivated state, suppressing repetitive firing. Pharmacokinetics: Zero-order (saturation) kinetics — a small dose rise causes a large jump in level; ~90% protein-bound and a potent CYP inducer; give IV as fosphenytoin to avoid cardiotoxicity. Adverse effects: Nystagmus (earliest sign), then ataxia and sedation; gingival hyperplasia, hirsutism, megaloblastic anaemia (folate), osteomalacia (vitamin D), and fetal hydantoin syndrome.

II.9Gabapentin

  • Mechanism of action
  • Binds the alpha-2-delta subunit of voltage-gated calcium channels, reducing calcium influx and thereby decreasing release of excitatory neurotransmitters (glutamate, norepinephrine, substance P).

  • Despite its name, gabapentin does NOT bind GABA receptors, is NOT converted to GABA, and does NOT alter GABA metabolism.

  • Pharmacokinetics

  • Absorbed via the L-amino acid transporter in the intestine — absorption is saturable (dose-dependent bioavailability decreases at higher doses).
  • NOT protein-bound, NOT metabolized — excreted unchanged by the kidneys.
  • No CYP interactions — very low drug interaction potential.

  • Dose must be adjusted in renal impairment.

  • Adverse effects

  • Sedation, dizziness, ataxia, fatigue.
  • Peripheral edema.
  • Weight gain.

  • Generally well tolerated with few serious adverse effects.

  • Oral exam pearls

  • “Does NOT act on GABA” — despite the name, the mechanism is calcium channel alpha-2-delta subunit binding.
  • No hepatic metabolism, no protein binding, no CYP interactions — ideal for polypharmacy patients.
  • Renally cleared — dose-adjust in kidney disease.
  • Also used for neuropathic pain and postherpetic neuralgia (major off-label use).

II.10Zolpidem, Zaleplon, Ramelteon, Melatonin

  • Mechanism of action
  • Zolpidem and zaleplon (“Z-drugs”): non-benzodiazepine hypnotics that selectively bind the BZ1 (omega-1) subunit of the GABA_A receptor (alpha-1 subunit), enhancing GABAergic inhibition. Selectivity for alpha-1 gives hypnotic effect without significant anxiolytic, muscle-relaxant, or anticonvulsant activity.
  • Ramelteon: selective melatonin MT1 and MT2 receptor agonist in the suprachiasmatic nucleus — promotes sleep onset by mimicking endogenous melatonin’s circadian signal.

  • Melatonin: endogenous hormone that acts on MT1/MT2 receptors to regulate the sleep-wake cycle.

  • Pharmacokinetics

  • Zolpidem: rapid onset; half-life ~2–3 h; available in extended-release for sleep maintenance. Metabolized by CYP3A4.
  • Zaleplon: ultra-short half-life (~1 h) — suitable only for sleep-onset insomnia; can be taken in the middle of the night if >=4 hours of sleep remain.
  • Ramelteon: rapid absorption; extensive first-pass metabolism (CYP1A2); half-life ~1–2.5 h; no abuse potential — not a controlled substance.

  • Melatonin: OTC supplement; variable bioavailability; short half-life (~30–50 min).

  • Indication

  • All: short-term treatment of insomnia (primarily sleep-onset insomnia).

  • Ramelteon: particularly useful in patients with a history of substance abuse (no abuse potential, no controlled-substance scheduling).

  • Adverse effects

  • Zolpidem: next-day drowsiness (especially extended-release), complex sleep behaviors (sleepwalking, sleep-driving, sleep-eating), amnesia; lower doses recommended for women (slower clearance).
  • Zaleplon: headache, dizziness; fewer residual effects due to ultra-short half-life.
  • Ramelteon: dizziness, fatigue; contraindicated with fluvoxamine (strong CYP1A2 inhibitor). No rebound insomnia, no dependence.

  • Melatonin: generally well tolerated; headache, drowsiness.

  • Oral exam pearls

  • Z-drugs are selective for the alpha-1 subunit → hypnotic without anxiolytic or muscle-relaxant effects.
  • Zaleplon’s ultra-short half-life (~1 h) = sleep-onset ONLY.
  • Ramelteon = no abuse potential, not a controlled substance — safe in substance abuse patients.
  • Zolpidem complex sleep behaviors (sleep-driving) is a boxed warning.

II.11Benzodiazepines

  • Mechanism of action
  • Bind the BZ (benzodiazepine) site on the GABA_A receptor — a site distinct from the GABA binding site.
  • Increase the FREQUENCY of chloride channel opening in response to GABA → enhanced inhibitory postsynaptic potential.
  • They are indirect GABA modulators: they require GABA to be present (no effect without GABA — this is why they are safer than barbiturates in overdose).

  • Contrast with barbiturates: barbiturates increase the DURATION of Cl- channel opening and can directly activate the channel at high doses.

  • Pharmacokinetics

  • Well absorbed orally; some given IV/IM (lorazepam, midazolam, diazepam).
  • Classified by duration of action:
    • Ultra-short: midazolam (procedural sedation).
    • Short-acting: triazolam, oxazepam, lorazepam.
    • Long-acting: diazepam, chlordiazepoxide, clonazepam, flurazepam.
  • Most undergo hepatic CYP-mediated oxidation then glucuronidation. LOT drugs (Lorazepam, Oxazepam, Temazepam) undergo direct glucuronidation — preferred in liver disease and the elderly.

  • Diazepam has active metabolites (desmethyldiazepam) with very long half-lives → accumulation.

  • Pharmacological effects

  • Anxiolytic, sedative-hypnotic, anticonvulsant, muscle relaxant, amnestic (anterograde amnesia).

  • Therapeutic uses

  • Anxiety disorders (generalized anxiety, panic disorder).
  • Insomnia (short-term).
  • Status epilepticus (IV lorazepam is first-line; IV diazepam is alternative).
  • Procedural sedation and pre-anesthesia (midazolam).
  • Muscle spasm (diazepam).
  • Alcohol withdrawal (long-acting: chlordiazepoxide or diazepam).

  • Anticonvulsant: clonazepam for myoclonic/absence seizures.

  • Dependence

  • Tolerance develops to sedative and hypnotic effects but less so to anxiolytic effects.
  • Physical dependence with chronic use — abrupt withdrawal causes rebound insomnia, anxiety, tremors, and rarely seizures.
  • Taper gradually to avoid withdrawal.
  • Psychological dependence can occur.

  • Flumazenil: competitive antagonist at the BZ binding site — reverses benzodiazepine overdose but can precipitate seizures in chronic BZ users or mixed overdoses.

  • Oral exam pearls

  • “Frequency” for benzos, “Duration” for barbiturates — the classic mnemonic.
  • LOT drugs (Lorazepam, Oxazepam, Temazepam) = direct glucuronidation, safe in liver disease.
  • Flumazenil reverses BZ overdose but risks seizures in dependent patients.
  • Safer than barbiturates in overdose — cannot directly open the Cl- channel without GABA.
Say it aloud — 30–60 sec

Mechanism: Bind the benzodiazepine site on GABA-A and increase the frequency of chloride channel opening (barbiturates increase duration); they need GABA present, so they’re safer in overdose. Pharmacokinetics: Classified by duration; the LOT drugs (lorazepam, oxazepam, temazepam) undergo direct glucuronidation and are safest in liver disease. Pharmacological effects: Anxiolytic, sedative-hypnotic, anticonvulsant, muscle relaxant and amnestic. Therapeutic uses: Anxiety, insomnia, status epilepticus (IV lorazepam), alcohol withdrawal, procedural sedation. Dependence: Tolerance and physical dependence develop; withdrawal causes rebound anxiety and seizures — taper; reverse overdose with flumazenil.

II.12Opioids: Classification and MOA

  • Classification
  • Strong agonists: morphine, heroin, meperidine, methadone, fentanyl, hydromorphone, oxycodone.
  • Moderate agonists: codeine, hydrocodone.
  • Mixed agonist-antagonists: pentazocine, nalbuphine, butorphanol (agonist at kappa, antagonist/partial agonist at mu).
  • Partial agonists: buprenorphine (partial agonist at mu, antagonist at kappa).

  • Antagonists: naloxone, naltrexone.

  • Mechanism of action

  • Bind opioid receptors (mu, kappa, delta — all Gi/Go-protein-coupled receptors):
    • Mu: supraspinal analgesia, euphoria, respiratory depression, miosis, physical dependence, constipation — the most clinically important receptor.
    • Kappa: spinal analgesia, sedation, dysphoria, miosis.
    • Delta: analgesia, modulation of mu-receptor activity.
  • Activation leads to: inhibition of adenylyl cyclase (decreased cAMP), opening of K+ channels (hyperpolarization), and closing of voltage-gated Ca2+ channels (reduced neurotransmitter release) → net inhibition of pain transmission.

  • Act at both presynaptic (reduce transmitter release) and postsynaptic (hyperpolarize) neurons.

  • Pharmacological effects

  • Analgesia: effective for both acute and chronic pain; alters pain perception AND emotional response to pain.
  • Euphoria: feeling of well-being and contentment (mu-mediated) — basis for abuse.
  • Respiratory depression: decreased sensitivity of brainstem respiratory centers to CO2 — most common cause of death in overdose.
  • Miosis: pinpoint pupils — diagnostic sign in overdose; virtually no tolerance develops.
  • Cough suppression: antitussive effect (codeine, dextromethorphan).
  • GI effects: decreased motility, increased tone → constipation (no tolerance develops); nausea/vomiting (CTZ stimulation).

  • Histamine release (morphine): vasodilation, hypotension, urticaria, bronchoconstriction.

  • Mu receptor = the key receptor for analgesia, euphoria, respiratory depression, and dependence.
  • Respiratory depression is the cause of death in opioid overdose.
  • Miosis and constipation — two effects to which tolerance does NOT develop.
  • Mixed agonist-antagonists can precipitate withdrawal in opioid-dependent patients.

II.13Opioids — Strong Agonists — Morphine: MOA and PK

  • Mechanism of action
  • Prototype opioid; full agonist at mu receptors (primary), with activity at kappa and delta receptors.
  • Activates Gi-protein signaling → decreases cAMP, opens K+ channels, closes Ca2+ channels → inhibits ascending pain pathways in the spinal cord dorsal horn and activates descending inhibitory pain pathways from the periaqueductal gray.

  • Alters both the perception of pain and the emotional response to it.

  • Pharmacokinetics

  • Absorption: well absorbed by all routes — oral, IM, SC, IV, intrathecal, epidural, rectal. Oral bioavailability is LOW (~25%) due to extensive first-pass metabolism.
  • Distribution: crosses the BBB slowly (low lipid solubility compared to fentanyl); accumulates in skeletal muscle, kidneys, liver, and lungs.
  • Metabolism: hepatic glucuronidation to two key metabolites:
    • Morphine-6-glucuronide (M6G): active metabolite, more potent analgesic than morphine — accumulates in renal failure and can cause prolonged respiratory depression.
    • Morphine-3-glucuronide (M3G): inactive (may be neuroexcitatory at high concentrations).
  • Excretion: renal; dose must be reduced in renal impairment due to M6G accumulation.

  • Half-life: ~2–3 hours (parent compound); M6G has a longer half-life.

  • Oral exam pearls

  • Low oral bioavailability (~25%) due to first-pass effect — IV dose is much lower than oral dose.
  • M6G is an ACTIVE metabolite — renal failure → M6G accumulation → prolonged toxicity.
  • Low lipophilicity compared to fentanyl → slower BBB penetration.
  • Glucuronidation (NOT CYP-mediated) — fewer drug interactions than expected.
Say it aloud — 30–60 sec

Mechanism: A full mu-receptor agonist; via Gi signalling it inhibits ascending pain pathways in the spinal dorsal horn and activates descending inhibition from the periaqueductal grey, altering pain perception and the emotional response to it. Pharmacokinetics: Well absorbed by all routes but low oral bioavailability (~25%) from first-pass metabolism; glucuronidated to active morphine-6-glucuronide, which accumulates in renal failure and can cause prolonged respiratory depression; relatively low lipid solubility, so slower BBB entry than fentanyl.

II.14Opioids — Strong Agonists — Morphine: Actions, Uses, Adverse Effects, Tolerance, Interactions

  • Actions (pharmacological effects)
  • Analgesia: highly effective for severe acute and chronic pain; acts on mu receptors in the CNS.
  • Euphoria: sense of well-being, contentment (drives abuse potential).
  • Respiratory depression: dose-dependent reduction in brainstem responsiveness to CO2.
  • Miosis: pupillary constriction via stimulation of the Edinger-Westphal nucleus (diagnostic in overdose).
  • GI: decreased motility, increased sphincter tone → severe constipation; nausea and vomiting (stimulates CTZ).
  • Cardiovascular: histamine release → vasodilation, orthostatic hypotension, bradycardia.

  • Other: urinary retention, biliary colic (contracts sphincter of Oddi), antitussive, immunosuppressive.

  • Therapeutic uses

  • Severe acute pain (trauma, surgery, MI — reduces cardiac preload, beneficial in pulmonary edema).
  • Chronic cancer pain.
  • Acute pulmonary edema (venodilation reduces preload).
  • Severe diarrhea (NOT first-line; loperamide preferred).

  • Pre-anesthetic medication.

  • Adverse effects

  • Respiratory depression (leading cause of death in overdose).
  • Constipation (manage with laxatives; methylnaltrexone for opioid-induced constipation).
  • Nausea and vomiting.
  • Sedation and cognitive impairment.
  • Urinary retention.
  • Histamine release → pruritus, urticaria, bronchospasm (caution in asthmatics).

  • Hypotension.

  • Tolerance and physical dependence

  • Tolerance: develops with repeated use — requires escalating doses for the same analgesic effect. Develops to analgesia, euphoria, respiratory depression, and sedation. Does NOT develop to miosis and constipation.
  • Physical dependence: manifests as a withdrawal syndrome upon abrupt discontinuation or administration of an antagonist — symptoms include lacrimation, rhinorrhea, yawning, sweating, gooseflesh (piloerection), diarrhea, abdominal cramps, muscle aches, insomnia, restlessness. Opioid withdrawal is intensely uncomfortable but generally NOT life-threatening (unlike alcohol or barbiturate withdrawal).

  • Cross-tolerance: exists among mu-agonist opioids.

  • Drug interactions

  • CNS depressants (alcohol, benzodiazepines, barbiturates, general anesthetics): synergistic respiratory depression — most dangerous interaction.
  • MAO inhibitors: severe, unpredictable reactions (especially with meperidine — serotonin syndrome, hyperpyrexia).
  • Mixed agonist-antagonists (pentazocine, nalbuphine): can precipitate withdrawal in morphine-dependent patients.

  • Anticholinergics: additive urinary retention and constipation.

  • Oral exam pearls

  • “No tolerance to miosis and constipation” — high-yield repeated exam fact.
  • Opioid withdrawal is uncomfortable but NOT fatal; alcohol and barbiturate withdrawal CAN be fatal.
  • Morphine is used in acute pulmonary edema (venodilation reduces preload).
  • CNS depressant combinations are the most dangerous interactions (synergistic respiratory depression).

II.15Opioid Antagonists: Naloxone and Naltrexone

  • Mechanism of action
  • Both are competitive antagonists at opioid receptors (mu, kappa, and delta), with highest affinity for mu receptors.
  • They displace opioid agonists from receptors, rapidly reversing all effects — analgesia, respiratory depression, euphoria, miosis.

  • No intrinsic agonist activity — no analgesic effect, no dependence potential, no respiratory depression when given alone.

  • Therapeutic uses

  • Naloxone:
    • Emergency reversal of opioid overdose (respiratory depression) — drug of choice for acute opioid toxicity.
    • Diagnosis of opioid dependence (precipitates withdrawal in dependent patients).
    • Combined with opioid agonists to deter abuse (e.g., buprenorphine/naloxone — Suboxone).
    • Available as IV, IM, SC, intranasal (Narcan nasal spray).

  • Naltrexone:

    • Maintenance therapy to prevent relapse in opioid addiction (taken orally, once daily, or as monthly IM depot — Vivitrol).
    • Alcohol use disorder (reduces craving and pleasurable effects of alcohol — approved indication).

  • Key differences

Route IV, IM, SC, intranasal Oral, IM depot
Duration Short (~30–90 min) Long (~24–72 h oral; 30 days IM)
Oral bioavailability Very low (extensive first-pass) Good oral bioavailability

  • Adverse effects
  • Both: can precipitate acute withdrawal syndrome in opioid-dependent patients (tachycardia, hypertension, nausea, vomiting, piloerection, pain).
  • Naloxone: short duration may require re-dosing — opioid effect can outlast naloxone (“re-narcotization”).

  • Naltrexone: hepatotoxicity at high doses (monitor LFTs); nausea, headache, anxiety.

  • Naltrexone = long-acting, oral/IM depot = maintenance and relapse prevention.

  • Naltrexone is also used for ALCOHOL dependence — not just opioids.

Adverse effects: Both precipitate withdrawal in dependent patients; naltrexone is hepatotoxic at high doses.

III.1Selection of Antimicrobial Therapy

  • Identifying the organism: Gram stain and culture are the gold standard; rapid tests (PCR, antigen detection) can guide early therapy. Specimen must be collected before starting antibiotics whenever possible.
  • Empiric therapy before ID: Based on the most likely pathogen for the clinical syndrome (e.g., community-acquired pneumonia → amoxicillin or azithromycin). Broad-spectrum agents are chosen, then de-escalated once culture and sensitivity return.
  • Determining susceptibility: Minimum inhibitory concentration (MIC) via broth dilution or Kirby-Bauer disk diffusion. Reported as susceptible, intermediate, or resistant. MIC guides drug and dose selection.
  • Effect of site of infection: Drug must reach the infected site at adequate concentrations. The blood-brain barrier (BBB) excludes many drugs — inflammation increases permeability (e.g., penicillin G penetrates inflamed meninges). Abscesses require drainage because antibiotics penetrate poorly into walled-off collections.
  • Patient factors: Age, renal/hepatic function, pregnancy/lactation, immune status, allergies, genetic polymorphisms (e.g., G6PD deficiency contraindicates sulfonamides), and presence of foreign bodies.
  • Safety of the agent: Always weigh efficacy against toxicity. Prefer the least toxic effective agent — e.g., avoid aminoglycosides if a safer alternative exists.

Oral exam pearls

  • “Empiric → culture → de-escalate” is the clinical mantra.
  • Inflamed meninges = increased BBB permeability — a favorite exam point.
  • Always collect cultures before the first dose.
  • Renal dosing adjustment is the most commonly tested patient factor.
Say it aloud — 30–60 sec

Identify the organism: Gram stain, culture and rapid tests — take samples before antibiotics. Empiric therapy: Start broad cover for the most likely pathogen, then de-escalate once cultures return. Susceptibility: The MIC shows susceptible, intermediate or resistant. Site of infection: The drug must reach it — many cross the blood-brain barrier only when meninges are inflamed, and abscesses need drainage. Patient factors: Age, renal/hepatic function, pregnancy, allergies, immune status and G6PD. Safety: Choose the least toxic effective agent.

III.2Determinants of Rational Dosing

  • Concentration-dependent killing: Bactericidal activity increases as drug concentration rises above the MIC. The key parameter is peak/MIC ratio (Cmax/MIC). Goal: give high doses, less frequently. Examples: aminoglycosides, fluoroquinolones, daptomycin.
  • Time-dependent (concentration-independent) killing: Efficacy depends on the duration the drug concentration stays above the MIC (T > MIC). Increasing concentration beyond a threshold does not increase killing. Goal: frequent dosing or continuous infusion. Examples: β-lactams (penicillins, cephalosporins, carbapenems), vancomycin.
  • Post-antibiotic effect (PAE): Persistent suppression of bacterial growth after drug levels fall below the MIC. Aminoglycosides and fluoroquinolones show a significant PAE against gram-negatives, allowing extended-interval dosing. β-Lactams generally have minimal PAE (except against gram-positives with carbapenems).

Oral exam pearls

  • “Aminoglycosides = concentration-dependent + long PAE → once-daily dosing.”
  • “β-Lactams = time-dependent + short PAE → frequent dosing or continuous infusion.”
  • PAE is the reason aminoglycosides can be dosed once daily despite short half-lives.
  • Fluoroquinolones are both concentration-dependent AND have a long PAE.
Say it aloud — 30–60 sec

Concentration-dependent killing: For aminoglycosides and fluoroquinolones a higher peak-to-MIC ratio kills more, so give large infrequent (once-daily) doses. Time-dependent killing: For beta-lactams and vancomycin what matters is the time the level stays above the MIC, so dose frequently or by continuous infusion — raising concentration doesn’t help. Post-antibiotic effect: Growth stays suppressed after levels fall below the MIC; aminoglycosides and fluoroquinolones have a long PAE, which is why once-daily dosing works.

III.3Chemotherapeutic Spectrum

  • Narrow-spectrum antibiotics: Active against a limited range of organisms. Useful when the pathogen is identified. Examples: isoniazid (Mycobacterium tuberculosis only), vancomycin (gram-positives only), penicillin G (mainly gram-positives and some gram-negatives).
  • Extended-spectrum antibiotics: Modified narrow-spectrum agents with activity against additional organisms. Examples: ampicillin and amoxicillin — retain gram-positive coverage of penicillin G but add some gram-negative coverage (H. influenzae, E. coli, Proteus).
  • Broad-spectrum antibiotics: Active against a wide range of both gram-positive and gram-negative organisms. Examples: tetracyclines, fluoroquinolones, carbapenems (broadest of β-lactams). Risk: increased disruption of normal flora → superinfection (e.g., C. difficile colitis, vaginal candidiasis).

Oral exam pearls

  • Narrow-spectrum is preferred when the pathogen is known — less collateral damage to flora.
  • Broad-spectrum agents increase the risk of superinfection and resistance selection.
  • Carbapenems are the broadest-spectrum β-lactams — “last resort” agents.
  • Extended-spectrum = aminopenicillins is a classic exam association.
Say it aloud — 30–60 sec

Narrow-spectrum: Active against a limited range (isoniazid, vancomycin, penicillin G) — best when the pathogen is known, sparing normal flora. Extended-spectrum: Aminopenicillins like ampicillin/amoxicillin keep gram-positive cover and add some gram-negatives. Broad-spectrum: Tetracyclines, fluoroquinolones and carbapenems hit many gram-positives and gram-negatives — useful empirically but they disrupt flora, risking superinfection like C. difficile colitis, and promote resistance.

III.4Combinations of Antimicrobial Therapy

  • Advantages:
  • Synergy: Combined effect is greater than the sum of individual effects (e.g., penicillin + gentamicin for enterococcal endocarditis; TMP + SMX for sequential folate pathway blockade).
  • Prevention of resistance: Multiple drugs make it harder for organisms to develop resistance (e.g., TB multi-drug regimen: isoniazid + rifampin + pyrazinamide + ethambutol).
  • Broadened coverage: In mixed or unknown infections (e.g., empiric therapy in febrile neutropenia).

  • Reduced toxicity: Lower doses of each drug may be used.

  • Disadvantages:

  • Antagonism: One drug reduces the effect of another (e.g., bacteriostatic tetracycline can antagonize bactericidal penicillin, which requires active cell division).
  • Increased cost and adverse effects.
  • Selection of multi-drug-resistant organisms.

  • False sense of security may delay definitive diagnosis.

  • Drug resistance:

  • Natural (intrinsic): Organism inherently lacks the target (e.g., Mycoplasma lacks a cell wall → resistant to β-lactams).
  • Acquired: Via mutation or horizontal gene transfer (plasmids, transposons, conjugation). Mechanisms include target modification, enzymatic drug inactivation (β-lactamases), decreased permeability (porin loss), and increased efflux pumps.

Oral exam pearls

  • “Bacteriostatic + bactericidal = potential antagonism” is a classic exam trap.
  • TB therapy is the #1 example of combination therapy to prevent resistance.
  • Synergy: penicillin damages wall → gentamicin enters more easily.
  • Resistance transfer via plasmids is the most clinically significant mechanism.
Say it aloud — 30–60 sec

Advantages: Synergy (penicillin + gentamicin in enterococcal endocarditis; TMP-SMX), prevention of resistance (TB uses isoniazid + rifampin + pyrazinamide + ethambutol), and broadened empiric cover. Disadvantages: Antagonism (a bacteriostatic drug like tetracycline blocks a bactericidal penicillin), higher cost, more side effects, and selection of resistant organisms. Resistance: Intrinsic (no target) or acquired by mutation or plasmid transfer — mechanisms are enzymatic inactivation, target modification, decreased permeability and efflux pumps.

III.5Prophylactic Antibiotics; Complications of Antibiotic Therapy

  • Prophylactic antibiotics:
  • Given before expected exposure to prevent infection, not to treat it.
  • Surgical prophylaxis: Single dose of cefazolin given 30–60 min before incision; covers skin flora (Staph/Strep). Continued for ≤24 h post-op.
  • Medical prophylaxis: TMP-SMX for Pneumocystis in immunocompromised; penicillin V for rheumatic fever recurrence; isoniazid for latent TB; azithromycin for MAC in AIDS.

  • Post-exposure: Rifampin or ciprofloxacin for meningococcal contacts.

  • Complications of antibiotic therapy:

  • Hypersensitivity: Most common with penicillins (type I–IV reactions). Cross-reactivity with cephalosporins ~1–2%.
  • Direct toxicity: Aminoglycoside nephro-/ototoxicity, chloramphenicol bone marrow suppression, fluoroquinolone tendon rupture.
  • Superinfection: Disruption of normal flora → C. difficile pseudomembranous colitis (treat with oral vancomycin or fidaxomicin), vaginal candidiasis.
  • Drug resistance: Overuse and misuse select for resistant organisms (MRSA, VRE, ESBL producers, CRE).

Oral exam pearls

  • Cefazolin is the go-to surgical prophylaxis drug — give it 30–60 min pre-incision.
  • C. difficile colitis is the most tested complication of broad-spectrum antibiotics.
  • Penicillin allergy is the most common drug allergy in medicine.
  • Prophylaxis ≠ treatment — short course, targeted, before exposure.
Say it aloud — 30–60 sec

III.6Penicillins

  • Definition/Intro: β-Lactam antibiotics; among the oldest and most widely used antimicrobials.

  • Mechanism of action:

  • Bind penicillin-binding proteins (PBPs) — transpeptidases that catalyze the final cross-linking step of peptidoglycan synthesis.
  • Inhibit transpeptidation → weakened cell wall → osmotic lysis and death.
  • Activate bacterial autolysins → accelerated cell wall degradation.

  • Bactericidal; require actively growing/dividing cells.

  • Antibacterial spectrum:

  • Penicillin G/V: Gram-positives (Strep, some Staph), Neisseria, Treponema pallidum, Actinomyces.
  • Aminopenicillins (ampicillin, amoxicillin): Extended gram-negative coverage — H. influenzae, E. coli, Proteus, Listeria, Enterococcus.
  • Antistaphylococcal (nafcillin, oxacillin, dicloxacillin): Penicillinase-resistant; for Staph aureus (not MRSA).

  • Antipseudomonal (piperacillin, ticarcillin): Broadest spectrum; used with β-lactamase inhibitors (piperacillin-tazobactam).

  • Resistance:

  • β-Lactamase production: Most common — enzymatic hydrolysis of β-lactam ring. Overcome by adding β-lactamase inhibitors (clavulanate, sulbactam, tazobactam).
  • Altered PBPs: MRSA produces PBP2a (encoded by mecA gene) → low affinity for all β-lactams.
  • Decreased permeability: Loss of porins in gram-negatives.

  • Efflux pumps.

  • Pharmacokinetics:

  • Penicillin G: IV/IM; acid-labile (destroyed by gastric acid). Penicillin V: oral, acid-stable.
  • Amoxicillin: Best oral absorption of all penicillins.
  • Distribution: Good tissue penetration; cross BBB only when meninges are inflamed.
  • Elimination: Renal (tubular secretion) — probenecid blocks secretion, prolonging half-life.

  • Short half-lives → frequent dosing required.

  • Adverse reactions:

  • Hypersensitivity (most important): Rash, urticaria, serum sickness, anaphylaxis (type I — most dangerous). ~1–2% cross-reactivity with cephalosporins.
  • Diarrhea and GI upset (especially ampicillin).
  • Ampicillin rash: Non-allergic maculopapular rash in patients with EBV mononucleosis (~100%).
  • Seizures at very high doses (especially with renal impairment).
  • Interstitial nephritis (methicillin — withdrawn for this reason).

Oral exam pearls

  • “Penicillin = PBP inhibitor → blocks transpeptidation → osmotic lysis.”
  • Amoxicillin = best oral bioavailability among penicillins.
  • Ampicillin + mononucleosis = guaranteed rash — not a true allergy.
  • MRSA resistance is due to altered PBP2a (mecA), not β-lactamase.
Say it aloud — 30–60 sec

Mechanism: Bactericidal — bind penicillin-binding proteins and block transpeptidation of the peptidoglycan wall, so the cell lyses. Spectrum: Penicillin G for gram-positives and syphilis; aminopenicillins add gram-negatives; antipseudomonals (piperacillin) are broadest. Resistance: Mainly beta-lactamase (overcome with clavulanate/tazobactam); altered PBP2a in MRSA. Pharmacokinetics: Renally excreted by tubular secretion (probenecid prolongs); amoxicillin has the best oral absorption. Adverse reactions: Hypersensitivity up to anaphylaxis, GI upset, and a non-allergic ampicillin rash in EBV mononucleosis.

III.7Cephalosporins

  • Definition/Intro: β-Lactam antibiotics structurally related to penicillins; classified by generation (1st–5th) with progressively broader gram-negative and narrowing gram-positive coverage.

  • Mechanism of action:

  • Same as penicillins: bind PBPs → inhibit transpeptidation of peptidoglycan → cell wall weakening → osmotic lysis.

  • Bactericidal; time-dependent killing.

  • Antibacterial spectrum (by generation):

  • 1st gen (cefazolin IV, cephalexin PO): Best gram-positive coverage (Staph, Strep). Surgical prophylaxis drug of choice.
  • 2nd gen (cefuroxime, cefoxitin, cefaclor): Increased gram-negative; cefoxitin covers anaerobes (including Bacteroides fragilis) — used in intra-abdominal infections.
  • 3rd gen (ceftriaxone, cefotaxime, ceftazidime): Strong gram-negative, cross BBB. Ceftriaxone = meningitis, gonorrhea. Ceftazidime = Pseudomonas.
  • 4th gen (cefepime): Broad gram-positive + gram-negative + Pseudomonas; resistant to many β-lactamases.

  • 5th gen (ceftaroline): Uniquely covers MRSA (binds PBP2a) + broad gram-negative. Only β-lactam active against MRSA.

  • Resistance:

  • β-Lactamase (cephalosporinase) production — especially extended-spectrum β-lactamases (ESBLs) in gram-negatives.
  • Altered PBPs (except ceftaroline can overcome PBP2a).

  • Decreased porin permeability.

  • Pharmacokinetics:

  • Variable oral absorption; many are parenteral only.
  • Ceftriaxone: Longest half-life → once-daily dosing; biliary excretion (can cause biliary sludge).
  • Most are renally excreted — dose-adjust in renal impairment (except ceftriaxone).

  • Good tissue penetration; 3rd gen and above cross inflamed BBB.

  • Adverse reactions:

  • Hypersensitivity: ~1–2% cross-reactivity with penicillin allergy; avoid in anaphylaxis history to penicillin.
  • Vitamin K deficiency/bleeding: Cephalosporins with MTT side chain (cefotetan, cefamandole) — inhibit vitamin K recycling.
  • Disulfiram-like reaction with alcohol (same MTT-containing drugs).
  • Ceftriaxone: Biliary sludge/pseudolithiasis — avoid in neonates (also displaces bilirubin → kernicterus risk).
  • C. difficile colitis (especially 3rd gen).

Oral exam pearls

  • “1st gen = gram-positive king; going up generations = more gram-negative, less gram-positive.”
  • Ceftriaxone: meningitis + gonorrhea + longest half-life + biliary sludge.
  • Ceftaroline is the ONLY β-lactam that kills MRSA.
  • MTT side chain = bleeding + disulfiram reaction — cefotetan is the classic example.
Say it aloud — 30–60 sec

Mechanism: Like penicillins, bind PBPs and block cell-wall synthesis. Spectrum by generation: 1st (cefazolin) best gram-positive and surgical prophylaxis; 2nd add gram-negatives/anaerobes; 3rd (ceftriaxone) for meningitis and gonorrhoea; 4th (cefepime) adds Pseudomonas; 5th (ceftaroline) is the only beta-lactam covering MRSA. III.8Other β-Lactams — Carbapenems and Monobactams

Carbapenems

  • Definition/Intro: Broadest-spectrum β-lactams; reserved for serious multi-drug-resistant infections.

  • Mechanism of action:

  • Bind PBPs → inhibit transpeptidation → cell wall lysis. Same core mechanism as all β-lactams.

  • Bactericidal; resistant to most β-lactamases (but NOT metallo-β-lactamases).

  • Antibacterial spectrum:

  • Imipenem, meropenem, doripenem: Broadest — gram-positives, gram-negatives (including Pseudomonas), and anaerobes. Cover ESBL-producing organisms.
  • Ertapenem: Same broad coverage BUT no Pseudomonas or Acinetobacter activity. Once-daily dosing.

  • None cover MRSA or atypicals.

  • Resistance:

  • Carbapenemases (e.g., KPC, NDM metallo-β-lactamases) — a growing threat (CRE: carbapenem-resistant Enterobacteriaceae).

  • Porin loss + efflux pumps in Pseudomonas.

  • Pharmacokinetics:

  • All parenteral (IV/IM). Renal elimination — dose-adjust in renal impairment.
  • Imipenem is co-administered with cilastatin — inhibits renal dehydropeptidase I (DHP-1) that would otherwise inactivate imipenem in the proximal tubule.
  • Meropenem, doripenem, ertapenem are stable to DHP-1 → no cilastatin needed.

  • Meropenem crosses BBB well → used for meningitis (imipenem is avoided — seizure risk).

  • Adverse reactions:

  • Seizures: Especially imipenem (highest risk) — avoid in meningitis and CNS infections; use meropenem instead.
  • GI disturbance (nausea, vomiting, diarrhea).
  • Hypersensitivity: Cross-reactivity with penicillins is low but possible.
  • C. difficile colitis.

Monobactams

  • Definition/Intro: Aztreonam is the only clinically used monobactam — a monocyclic β-lactam.

  • Mechanism of action:

  • Binds PBP3 of gram-negative aerobes only → inhibits cell wall synthesis. Bactericidal.

  • Antibacterial spectrum:

  • Gram-negative aerobes only (including Pseudomonas aeruginosa).

  • No activity against gram-positives or anaerobes.

  • Resistance: Susceptible to metallo-β-lactamases and some ESBLs.

  • Pharmacokinetics:

  • IV/IM administration. Renal excretion.

  • Penetrates inflamed BBB.

  • Adverse reactions:

  • Generally well-tolerated.
  • No cross-reactivity with penicillins or cephalosporins — safe in penicillin-allergic patients (different ring structure).
  • GI upset, rash rarely.

Oral exam pearls

  • Imipenem = always with cilastatin; causes seizures → never for meningitis.
  • Meropenem = safe for meningitis (no seizures, crosses BBB).
  • Ertapenem = the carbapenem that does NOT cover Pseudomonas.
  • Aztreonam = gram-negative aerobes only + SAFE in penicillin allergy (zero cross-reactivity).
Say it aloud — 30–60 sec

Carbapenems (imipenem, meropenem, ertapenem): Bactericidal PBP binders and the broadest beta-lactams — gram-positives, gram-negatives and anaerobes — resisting most beta-lactamases. Imipenem is given with cilastatin (blocks renal DHP-1) and has the highest seizure risk (use meropenem for meningitis); ertapenem lacks Pseudomonas cover; resistance via carbapenemases (CRE). Monobactam (aztreonam): Binds PBP3 of gram-negative aerobes only (including Pseudomonas), no gram-positive/anaerobe cover, and has no cross-reactivity with penicillins — safe in penicillin allergy.

III.9Vancomycin, Daptomycin, Telavancin

Vancomycin

  • Definition/Intro: Glycopeptide antibiotic; the cornerstone for serious gram-positive infections including MRSA.

  • Mechanism of action:

  • Binds D-Ala-D-Ala terminus of peptidoglycan precursors → blocks transglycosylation and transpeptidation → inhibits cell wall synthesis.
  • Bactericidal (slowly); time-dependent killing.

  • Does NOT bind PBPs — works at a different step than β-lactams.

  • Spectrum and uses:

  • Gram-positives only: MRSA, MRSE, Enterococcus (E. faecalis, not VRE), Streptococcus pneumoniae (penicillin-resistant), C. difficile (oral only).

  • IV for systemic MRSA infections; oral vancomycin for C. difficile colitis (not absorbed — acts locally in gut).

  • Adverse reactions:

  • Red man syndrome: Histamine-mediated flushing/erythema of upper body from rapid IV infusion — NOT an allergy. Prevented by slow infusion and pre-treatment with antihistamines.
  • Nephrotoxicity (especially with aminoglycosides).
  • Ototoxicity (less common; risk increases with aminoglycoside co-administration).
  • Thrombophlebitis at infusion site.

Daptomycin

  • Definition/Intro: Cyclic lipopeptide antibiotic for resistant gram-positive infections.

  • Mechanism of action:

  • Inserts into the gram-positive cell membrane → forms ion channels → rapid depolarization → loss of membrane potential → cell death.

  • Bactericidal; concentration-dependent killing.

  • Spectrum and uses:

  • Gram-positives including MRSA, VRE (E. faecium).
  • Complicated skin infections, MRSA bacteremia, right-sided endocarditis.

  • NOT for pneumonia — inactivated by pulmonary surfactant.

  • Adverse reactions:

  • Myopathy/rhabdomyolysis: Monitor CPK weekly. Discontinue statins during therapy (additive myotoxicity).
  • Eosinophilic pneumonia (rare).

Telavancin

  • Definition/Intro: Lipoglycopeptide (vancomycin derivative) with a dual mechanism.

  • Mechanism of action:

  • Binds D-Ala-D-Ala (like vancomycin) AND disrupts cell membrane integrity → dual bactericidal action.

  • Spectrum and uses:

  • Complicated skin infections caused by gram-positives including MRSA.

  • Hospital-acquired pneumonia (gram-positive).

  • Adverse reactions:

  • Teratogenic — pregnancy test required before use.
  • Nephrotoxicity, taste disturbance, QTc prolongation, foamy urine.

Oral exam pearls

  • Red man syndrome = rate-related histamine release, not allergy — slow the infusion.
  • Oral vancomycin is NOT absorbed — used ONLY for C. difficile (not systemic infections).
  • Daptomycin is inactivated by surfactant → NEVER use for pneumonia.
  • Daptomycin + statins = myopathy risk → stop statins.
Say it aloud — 30–60 sec

Vancomycin: A glycopeptide binding the D-ala-D-ala terminus to block cell-wall synthesis; covers gram-positives including MRSA (IV) and C. difficile (oral, not absorbed). Fast infusion causes red-man syndrome (histamine, not allergy); also nephro/ototoxic. Daptomycin: A lipopeptide that depolarises the membrane; covers MRSA and VRE but is inactivated by surfactant so is NEVER used for pneumonia — monitor CK for myopathy and stop statins. Telavancin: A lipoglycopeptide with dual action (wall + membrane) — teratogenic, needs a pregnancy test.

III.10Tetracyclines

  • Definition/Intro: Broad-spectrum bacteriostatic antibiotics; include tetracycline, doxycycline, minocycline, and the glycylcycline tigecycline.

  • Mechanism of action:

  • Bind 30S ribosomal subunit → block attachment of aminoacyl-tRNA to the A site of the mRNA-ribosome complex → inhibit protein synthesis.

  • Bacteriostatic.

  • Antibacterial spectrum:

  • Broad: gram-positives, gram-negatives, atypicals (Mycoplasma, Chlamydia, Rickettsia), spirochetes (Borrelia burgdorferi — Lyme disease).
  • Doxycycline: Drug of choice for Lyme disease, Rocky Mountain spotted fever (Rickettsia), Chlamydia, cholera, MRSA skin infections (community-acquired).

  • Tigecycline: Covers MRSA, VRE, ESBL producers, anaerobes.

  • Resistance:

  • Decreased accumulation: Plasmid-encoded efflux pumps (most common) and decreased membrane permeability.
  • Ribosomal protection proteins: Displace tetracycline from the 30S subunit.

  • Tigecycline overcomes many traditional tetracycline resistance mechanisms.

  • Pharmacokinetics:

  • Oral absorption impaired by divalent/trivalent cations (Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺, Fe²⁺, Al³⁺) — chelation. Avoid dairy, antacids, iron supplements.
  • Doxycycline: Exception — absorption less affected by food; eliminated via feces/bile (safe in renal failure, unlike other tetracyclines).
  • Accumulate in bones and teeth (chelate calcium).

  • Good intracellular penetration (effective against intracellular organisms).

  • Adverse reactions:

  • Teeth discoloration (permanent yellow-brown) and bone growth impairment in children — contraindicated in children < 8 years and pregnancy.
  • Photosensitivity (especially demeclocycline).
  • GI disturbance (nausea, diarrhea, esophageal ulceration — take with water, upright).
  • Fanconi syndrome: Outdated/degraded tetracyclines → renal tubular damage.
  • Hepatotoxicity (especially IV, pregnancy).
  • Vestibular toxicity (minocycline — vertigo, dizziness).
  • Demeclocycline: Used therapeutically for SIADH (causes nephrogenic DI).

Oral exam pearls

  • “30S, A site, bacteriostatic” — the ribosomal mnemonic for tetracyclines.
  • Avoid in children < 8 and pregnancy — teeth/bone effects.
  • Doxycycline is safe in renal failure (biliary excretion) — the exception among tetracyclines.
  • Chelation by cations = do not take with milk, antacids, or iron.
Say it aloud — 30–60 sec

Mechanism: Bacteriostatic — bind the 30S subunit and block aminoacyl-tRNA from the A site. Spectrum: Broad, including atypicals — doxycycline is first-line for Lyme, Rickettsia and Chlamydia. Resistance: Efflux pumps and ribosomal protection proteins. Pharmacokinetics: Absorption blocked by divalent cations (avoid dairy, antacids, iron); doxycycline is biliary-cleared, so safe in renal failure. Adverse reactions: Tooth discoloration and impaired bone growth (avoid under age 8 and in pregnancy), photosensitivity, and Fanconi syndrome from expired drug.

III.11Aminoglycosides

  • Definition/Intro: Bactericidal antibiotics primarily for serious gram-negative infections; include gentamicin, tobramycin, amikacin, streptomycin, neomycin.

  • Mechanism of action:

  • Bind irreversibly to 30S ribosomal subunit (specifically the 16S rRNA of the 30S) → cause misreading of mRNA and premature termination → produce faulty proteins that insert into the bacterial membrane → membrane damage → cell death.
  • Bactericidal (unusual for protein synthesis inhibitors — most are bacteriostatic).
  • Concentration-dependent killing with a long post-antibiotic effect → once-daily dosing.

  • Require oxygen-dependent transport to enter cells → inactive against anaerobes and in anaerobic environments (abscesses).

  • Antibacterial spectrum:

  • Primarily aerobic gram-negatives (Enterobacteriaceae, Pseudomonas).
  • Synergistic with β-lactams/vancomycin against gram-positives (Strep, Staph, Enterococcus — especially in endocarditis).
  • Streptomycin: Used in TB and plague.

  • Neomycin: Too toxic for systemic use — topical or oral (bowel decontamination, hepatic encephalopathy).

  • Resistance:

  • Enzymatic modification: Acetyltransferases, phosphotransferases, nucleotidyltransferases (plasmid-mediated — most common).
  • Decreased uptake (altered transport).

  • Modified ribosomal target (rare).

  • Pharmacokinetics:

  • Not absorbed orally (polycationic — can’t cross membranes) → IV or IM for systemic use.
  • Distribute to extracellular fluid; poor CNS penetration.
  • Renal excretion (unchanged) — dose-adjust in renal impairment. Monitor drug levels (trough levels for toxicity; peak levels for efficacy).

  • Once-daily (extended-interval) dosing exploits concentration-dependent killing and PAE.

  • Adverse reactions:

  • Nephrotoxicity: Reversible; accumulates in renal tubular cells. Risk increases with duration, trough levels, and concurrent nephrotoxins (vancomycin, amphotericin B).
  • Ototoxicity: Irreversible; cochlear (hearing loss — amikacin, neomycin) or vestibular (vertigo — gentamicin, streptomycin). Can be potentiated by loop diuretics.
  • Neuromuscular blockade: Can potentiate effects of neuromuscular blocking agents; risk in myasthenia gravis. Due to decreased acetylcholine release and postsynaptic receptor sensitivity.
  • Teratogenic: Contraindicated in pregnancy (fetal CN VIII damage).

Oral exam pearls

  • “Bactericidal protein synthesis inhibitor” — aminoglycosides are the exception to the static rule.
  • Nephrotoxicity is reversible; ototoxicity is irreversible — critical distinction.
  • No anaerobic activity — requires O2 for uptake.
  • “Once daily = exploit concentration-dependent killing + long PAE.”
Say it aloud — 30–60 sec

Mechanism: Bactericidal — irreversibly bind the 30S subunit causing mRNA misreading; uptake is oxygen-dependent, so inactive against anaerobes. Spectrum: Aerobic gram-negatives; synergistic with beta-lactams against gram-positives. Resistance: Plasmid-mediated modifying enzymes. Pharmacokinetics: Not absorbed orally — given IV/IM, renally cleared, dosed once daily (concentration-dependent killing plus long PAE) with level monitoring. Adverse reactions: Reversible nephrotoxicity, irreversible ototoxicity, and neuromuscular blockade; teratogenic.

III.12Macrolides

  • Definition/Intro: Bacteriostatic antibiotics with excellent atypical coverage; include erythromycin, azithromycin, clarithromycin.

  • Mechanism of action:

  • Bind 50S ribosomal subunit → block translocation step (movement of ribosome along mRNA) → inhibit protein synthesis.

  • Bacteriostatic (can be bactericidal at high concentrations against certain organisms).

  • Antibacterial spectrum:

  • Gram-positives (Strep, Staph — not MRSA), some gram-negatives.
  • Atypicals: Mycoplasma, Chlamydia, Legionella — first-line for “atypical pneumonia.”
  • Azithromycin: Also covers H. influenzae, Moraxella; used for community-acquired pneumonia, Chlamydia (single 1g dose), MAC prophylaxis in AIDS.
  • Clarithromycin: Part of H. pylori triple therapy; MAC treatment.

  • Erythromycin: Also a prokinetic agent (motilin receptor agonist).

  • Resistance:

  • Methylation of 23S rRNA on the 50S subunit (erm genes — most common) → decreased binding. Can confer cross-resistance to clindamycin and streptogramins (MLS_B resistance).
  • Efflux pumps (mef genes).

  • Enzymatic inactivation (esterases — rare).

  • Pharmacokinetics:

  • Excellent oral absorption (azithromycin and clarithromycin better tolerated than erythromycin).
  • Azithromycin: Longest half-life among macrolides; accumulates intracellularly (especially in phagocytes) → tissue levels far exceed plasma. 5-day course = 10 days of coverage. Biliary excretion.
  • Erythromycin and clarithromycin: Potent CYP3A4 inhibitors → many drug interactions (warfarin, statins, carbamazepine, theophylline, cyclosporine). Azithromycin has minimal CYP interactions.

  • Good intracellular penetration (key for atypicals).

  • Adverse reactions:

  • GI disturbance: Most common — nausea, vomiting, diarrhea (worst with erythromycin — motilin agonism stimulates GI motility).
  • QTc prolongation → risk of torsades de pointes (all macrolides, but especially erythromycin IV).
  • Cholestatic hepatitis (erythromycin estolate).
  • Ototoxicity: Reversible, high-dose.
  • Acute pyloric stenosis in neonates given erythromycin.

Oral exam pearls

  • “50S, translocation, bacteriostatic” — the macrolide mnemonic.
  • Azithromycin = long half-life, intracellular accumulation, minimal CYP inhibition.
  • Erythromycin = strong CYP3A4 inhibitor + motilin agonist (prokinetic + GI side effects).
  • MLS_B resistance = cross-resistance between macrolides, lincosamides (clindamycin), and streptogramins.
Say it aloud — 30–60 sec

Mechanism: Bacteriostatic — bind the 50S subunit and block translocation. Spectrum: Gram-positives and the atypicals (Mycoplasma, Chlamydia, Legionella) — first-line for atypical pneumonia. Resistance: Methylation of 23S rRNA (erm, gives MLS-B cross-resistance) and efflux pumps. Pharmacokinetics: Azithromycin has a long half-life, accumulates intracellularly and has few interactions; erythromycin and clarithromycin inhibit CYP3A4. Adverse reactions: GI upset (erythromycin is a motilin agonist), QT prolongation, and cholestatic hepatitis.

III.13Chloramphenicol

  • Definition/Intro: Broad-spectrum bacteriostatic antibiotic; limited use due to serious bone marrow toxicity. Reserved for serious infections when safer alternatives are unavailable.

  • Mechanism of action:

  • Binds 50S ribosomal subunit → inhibits peptidyl transferase → blocks peptide bond formation → inhibits protein synthesis.

  • Bacteriostatic (bactericidal against H. influenzae, N. meningitidis, S. pneumoniae).

  • Antibacterial spectrum:

  • Broad: gram-positives, gram-negatives, anaerobes, Rickettsia.
  • Historically used for bacterial meningitis (H. influenzae, N. meningitidis, S. pneumoniae) and typhoid fever (Salmonella typhi) — now largely replaced by ceftriaxone.

  • Still used for rickettsial infections in patients who cannot take tetracyclines (e.g., pregnant women, children).

  • Resistance:

  • Plasmid-mediated acetyltransferase → enzymatic inactivation (acetylation of the drug). Most common mechanism.

  • Pharmacokinetics:

  • Excellent oral absorption. Good penetration into CSF (even without inflammation) and most tissues.
  • Hepatic metabolism (glucuronidation).

  • CYP450 inhibitor → increases levels of warfarin, phenytoin.

  • Adverse reactions:

  • Dose-dependent bone marrow suppression: Reversible; related to mitochondrial protein synthesis inhibition. Common.
  • Idiosyncratic aplastic anemia: Rare (~1:20,000–40,000) but fatal and irreversible. Not dose-related. The reason chloramphenicol use is restricted.
  • Gray baby syndrome: In neonates — immature hepatic glucuronidation + immature renal excretion → drug accumulates → cardiovascular collapse (ashen-gray color, abdominal distension, shock). Due to inability to conjugate/excrete the drug.
  • GI upset.

Oral exam pearls

  • Two types of marrow toxicity: dose-dependent (reversible) vs. idiosyncratic aplastic anemia (irreversible, fatal).
  • Gray baby syndrome = neonatal glucuronidation deficiency → chloramphenicol accumulation.
  • “50S, peptidyl transferase” distinguishes it from macrolides (50S, translocation).
  • Resistance = acetyltransferase (same enzyme name, different drug from aminoglycoside acetyltransferases).
Say it aloud — 30–60 sec

Mechanism: Bacteriostatic — binds the 50S subunit and inhibits peptidyl transferase. Spectrum: Broad, with excellent CSF penetration even without inflammation. Resistance: Plasmid-encoded acetyltransferase that inactivates the drug. Pharmacokinetics: Good oral absorption; hepatic glucuronidation; a CYP inhibitor. Adverse reactions: Dose-dependent reversible bone-marrow suppression; rare fatal idiosyncratic aplastic anaemia; and gray baby syndrome in neonates, who can’t glucuronidate it.

III.14Fluoroquinolones

  • Definition/Intro: Bactericidal broad-spectrum antibiotics; include ciprofloxacin, levofloxacin, moxifloxacin, norfloxacin.

  • Mechanism of action:

  • Inhibit bacterial DNA gyrase (topoisomerase II) (primary target in gram-negatives) and topoisomerase IV (primary target in gram-positives).
  • Block DNA supercoiling, replication, and repair → bactericidal.

  • Concentration-dependent killing with a long post-antibiotic effect.

  • Antibacterial spectrum:

  • Ciprofloxacin: Best gram-negative coverage, including Pseudomonas aeruginosa. Anthrax prophylaxis/treatment.
  • Levofloxacin (“respiratory fluoroquinolone”): Gram-negatives + enhanced gram-positive coverage (S. pneumoniae) → community-acquired pneumonia.
  • Moxifloxacin: Broadest coverage — gram-positives + gram-negatives + anaerobes + atypicals. No Pseudomonas. No renal excretion → not for UTIs.

  • All cover atypicals (Mycoplasma, Chlamydia, Legionella).

  • Resistance:

  • Chromosomal mutations in DNA gyrase or topoisomerase IV genes (most common).
  • Efflux pumps.

  • Plasmid-mediated quinolone resistance (Qnr proteins that protect topoisomerases).

  • Pharmacokinetics:

  • Excellent oral bioavailability (oral ≈ IV).
  • Good tissue penetration; levofloxacin concentrates in lungs and urinary tract.
  • Chelation by divalent/trivalent cations (Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺, Al³⁺, Fe²⁺) — same as tetracyclines, avoid antacids.

  • Renal excretion (except moxifloxacin — hepatic/fecal).

  • Adverse reactions:

  • Tendinopathy/tendon rupture: Especially Achilles tendon. Risk increased with age > 60, corticosteroid use, renal failure.
  • QTc prolongation → torsades de pointes (especially moxifloxacin).
  • Cartilage damage in weight-bearing joints → contraindicated in children < 18 and pregnancy.
  • CNS effects: Headache, dizziness, insomnia, rarely seizures (lower seizure threshold).
  • C. difficile colitis.
  • Phototoxicity.
  • FDA black box warning: Tendinitis, peripheral neuropathy, CNS effects, aortic dissection.

Oral exam pearls

  • “DNA gyrase (gram-neg) + topoisomerase IV (gram-pos) = fluoroquinolone targets.”
  • Ciprofloxacin = Pseudomonas + anthrax; moxifloxacin = broadest but no Pseudomonas.
  • Contraindicated in children and pregnancy — cartilage damage.
  • Tendon rupture is the hallmark adverse effect — especially with steroids + age > 60.
Say it aloud — 30–60 sec

Mechanism: Bactericidal — inhibit DNA gyrase (gram-negatives) and topoisomerase IV (gram-positives), blocking DNA replication. Spectrum: Ciprofloxacin for gram-negatives and Pseudomonas; levofloxacin and moxifloxacin add gram-positive and atypical cover. Resistance: Chromosomal mutations in the target enzymes and efflux pumps. Pharmacokinetics: Excellent oral bioavailability (oral ≈ IV); chelated by cations. Adverse reactions: Tendon rupture, QT prolongation, CNS effects and cartilage damage — contraindicated in children and pregnancy (black-box warnings).

III.15Sulfonamides

  • Definition/Intro: Bacteriostatic antibiotics that are structural analogs of para-aminobenzoic acid (PABA); include sulfamethoxazole, sulfadiazine, sulfisoxazole.

  • Mechanism of action:

  • Competitively inhibit dihydropteroate synthase → block the conversion of PABA to dihydrofolic acid → inhibit folate synthesis → decreased thymidine and purine synthesis → inhibit DNA/RNA synthesis.
  • Bacteriostatic.

  • Selective toxicity: Humans obtain folate from diet; bacteria must synthesize it.

  • Antibacterial spectrum:

  • Broad: gram-positives, gram-negatives, Nocardia, Chlamydia trachomatis, some protozoa (Toxoplasma — sulfadiazine + pyrimethamine).

  • Often used in combination with trimethoprim (see topic 17).

  • Resistance:

  • Overproduction of PABA.
  • Mutation in dihydropteroate synthase → decreased drug affinity.

  • Decreased permeability to sulfonamides.

  • Pharmacokinetics:

  • Well absorbed orally. Wide tissue distribution; cross placenta and BBB.
  • Hepatic metabolism (acetylation — acetylated metabolites are poorly soluble → crystalluria).
  • Renal excretion.

  • Compete with bilirubin for albumin binding → displace bilirubin → kernicterus in neonates.

  • Adverse reactions:

  • Hypersensitivity: Rash, Stevens-Johnson syndrome (SJS), toxic epidermal necrolysis (TEN).
  • Crystalluria/nephrotoxicity: Acetylated metabolites precipitate in acidic urine. Prevent with adequate hydration and urine alkalinization.
  • Kernicterus: In neonates — displaces bilirubin from albumin → contraindicated in neonates and late pregnancy.
  • Hemolytic anemia in G6PD-deficient patients (oxidative stress).
  • Bone marrow suppression (megaloblastic anemia — folate deficiency).
  • Photosensitivity.
  • Drug interactions: Displace warfarin, methotrexate, sulfonylureas from albumin → potentiate their effects.

Oral exam pearls

  • “PABA analog → inhibits dihydropteroate synthase → blocks folate synthesis.”
  • Crystalluria prevention: hydrate + alkalinize urine.
  • Contraindicated in neonates/late pregnancy (kernicterus) and G6PD deficiency (hemolysis).
  • SJS/TEN is the most feared hypersensitivity reaction.
Say it aloud — 30–60 sec

Mechanism: Bacteriostatic PABA analogs that competitively inhibit dihydropteroate synthase, blocking folate synthesis (selective, since bacteria must make folate). Spectrum: Broad; often combined with trimethoprim. Resistance: Overproduction of PABA or an altered enzyme. Pharmacokinetics: Well absorbed, cross the placenta; acetylated metabolites can crystallise. Adverse reactions: Stevens-Johnson syndrome, crystalluria (hydrate/alkalinise urine), haemolysis in G6PD deficiency, and kernicterus — contraindicated in neonates and late pregnancy.

III.16Trimethoprim

  • Definition/Intro: Bacteriostatic antibiotic that inhibits a different step in folate synthesis than sulfonamides; most commonly used in combination with sulfamethoxazole (TMP-SMX).

  • Mechanism of action:

  • Inhibits dihydrofolate reductase (DHFR) → blocks conversion of dihydrofolic acid to tetrahydrofolic acid (THF) → decreased thymidine synthesis → inhibited DNA synthesis.
  • Bacteriostatic alone; bactericidal in combination with sulfamethoxazole (sequential blockade of folate pathway).

  • Selective toxicity: Trimethoprim has ~50,000–100,000x higher affinity for bacterial DHFR than human DHFR.

  • Antibacterial spectrum:

  • Similar to sulfonamides. Gram-positives, gram-negatives.

  • Alone: used for uncomplicated UTIs.

  • Resistance:

  • Altered/overproduced dihydrofolate reductase (plasmid-mediated — most common).

  • Decreased permeability.

  • Pharmacokinetics:

  • Well absorbed orally. Good tissue distribution; concentrates in prostatic and vaginal fluids (useful for UTIs and prostatitis).
  • Crosses BBB.
  • Renal excretion (partially via tubular secretion).

  • Weakly inhibits renal tubular secretion of creatinine → may elevate serum creatinine without actual renal damage (not true nephrotoxicity).

  • Adverse reactions:

  • Megaloblastic anemia (folate deficiency) — especially in folate-depleted patients (pregnancy, alcoholism, malnutrition). Can be reversed with folinic acid (leucovorin) — NOT folic acid.
  • Hyperkalemia: Blocks ENaC sodium channels in the collecting duct (amiloride-like effect).
  • Elevated creatinine (without true nephrotoxicity — see above).
  • Teratogenic (folate antagonist) → contraindicated in first trimester and late pregnancy.

Oral exam pearls

  • “TMP = DHFR inhibitor → blocks dihydrofolate → tetrahydrofolate step.”
  • Elevated creatinine from TMP is NOT nephrotoxicity — it inhibits tubular secretion of creatinine.
  • Megaloblastic anemia rescued with leucovorin (folinic acid), not folic acid.
  • TMP causes hyperkalemia via ENaC blockade — frequently tested.
Say it aloud — 30–60 sec

Mechanism: Inhibits dihydrofolate reductase (the step after sulfonamides), blocking folate and DNA synthesis; highly selective for the bacterial enzyme. Spectrum: Similar to sulfonamides; alone it treats uncomplicated UTIs. Resistance: Altered or overproduced dihydrofolate reductase. Pharmacokinetics: Concentrates in prostatic and vaginal fluid; renally excreted. Adverse reactions: Megaloblastic anaemia (rescue with leucovorin), hyperkalaemia (blocks ENaC like amiloride), and a harmless rise in creatinine from blocked tubular secretion — not true nephrotoxicity.

III.17Co-trimoxazole (TMP-SMX)

  • Definition/Intro: Fixed-dose combination of trimethoprim (TMP) + sulfamethoxazole (SMX) in a 1:5 ratio; achieves synergistic bactericidal activity through sequential folate pathway blockade.

  • Mechanism of action:

  • SMX inhibits dihydropteroate synthase (PABA → dihydrofolic acid — step 1).
  • TMP inhibits dihydrofolate reductase (dihydrofolic acid → tetrahydrofolic acid — step 2).

  • Sequential (double) blockade of folate synthesis → synergistic and bactericidal (each drug alone is bacteriostatic).

  • Antibacterial spectrum:

  • Broad: gram-positives, gram-negatives, Nocardia.
  • Drug of choice for:
    • Pneumocystis jirovecii pneumonia (PCP) — treatment and prophylaxis in HIV/AIDS.
    • Uncomplicated UTIs (E. coli).
    • MRSA skin/soft tissue infections (community-acquired).
    • Nocardia infections.
    • Toxoplasma (alternative to sulfadiazine-pyrimethamine).

  • NOT effective against Pseudomonas, Mycoplasma, or Treponema.

  • Resistance:

  • Combination of resistance mechanisms of both components: altered DHFR, altered dihydropteroate synthase, PABA overproduction, decreased permeability.

  • Sequential blockade reduces — but does not eliminate — resistance emergence.

  • Pharmacokinetics:

  • Both components well absorbed orally; similar half-lives (important for maintaining synergistic ratio).
  • Wide distribution; both cross BBB and placenta.
  • Hepatic metabolism + renal excretion.

  • Same albumin displacement issues as sulfonamides (warfarin, bilirubin, sulfonylureas).

  • Adverse reactions:

  • Combines adverse effects of both components:
    • Hypersensitivity/SJS/TEN (sulfonamide component — especially common in HIV patients — up to 50%).
    • Megaloblastic anemia (TMP — folate antagonism); treat with leucovorin.
    • Crystalluria (SMX) — hydrate and alkalinize urine.
    • Hyperkalemia (TMP — ENaC blockade).
    • Kernicterus in neonates (SMX — bilirubin displacement).
    • Hemolytic anemia in G6PD deficiency.
  • Contraindicated in: neonates, late pregnancy, G6PD deficiency, severe hepatic/renal impairment.

Oral exam pearls

  • “Double blockade of folate pathway = synergistic bactericidal” — the core concept.
  • PCP prophylaxis and treatment in HIV = TMP-SMX. If allergic, use dapsone or atovaquone.
  • HIV patients have a very high rate of sulfonamide hypersensitivity (~50%).
  • TMP-SMX is the oral MRSA drug for community-acquired skin infections.
Say it aloud — 30–60 sec

Mechanism: Sulfamethoxazole blocks dihydropteroate synthase and trimethoprim blocks dihydrofolate reductase — a sequential double blockade that is synergistic and bactericidal. Spectrum: Broad; the drug of choice for Pneumocystis jirovecii pneumonia (treatment and prophylaxis), plus UTIs, community-acquired MRSA and Nocardia. Resistance: Combined resistance of both components. Pharmacokinetics: Both well absorbed with matched half-lives; cross the placenta. Adverse reactions: Both drugs’ effects — hypersensitivity/SJS (very common in HIV), megaloblastic anaemia, hyperkalaemia and crystalluria; contraindicated in neonates, late pregnancy and G6PD deficiency.

IV.1Treatment of Hyperthyroidism

Mechanism of action

  • Thioamides (Methimazole, PTU): inhibit thyroid peroxidase (TPO), blocking oxidation and organification of iodide and coupling of iodotyrosines → decreased T3/T4 synthesis. PTU additionally inhibits peripheral conversion of T4→T3 by blocking 5’-deiodinase.
  • Iodides (Lugol’s solution, potassium iodide): at high doses, temporarily inhibit organification (Wolff-Chaikoff effect) and reduce thyroid vascularity; used pre-operatively to firm the gland and decrease surgical bleeding.
  • Radioactive iodine (I-131): taken up by thyroid follicular cells → emits beta particles → destroys thyroid tissue. Preferred definitive treatment in non-pregnant adults.
  • Beta-blockers (Propranolol): control sympathetic symptoms (tachycardia, tremor, anxiety). Propranolol also inhibits peripheral T4→T3 conversion at high doses.

Therapeutic uses

  • Methimazole: first-line thioamide for Graves’ disease (once-daily dosing, fewer side effects)
  • PTU: preferred only in first trimester of pregnancy (less teratogenic than methimazole) and in thyroid storm (blocks peripheral conversion)
  • Iodides: pre-surgical preparation; thyroid storm (given after thioamide)
  • I-131: definitive therapy for Graves’ disease; contraindicated in pregnancy
  • Propranolol: rapid symptom control while awaiting thioamide effect (takes weeks)

Adverse effects

  • Thioamides: agranulocytosis (rare but serious — instruct patient to report sore throat/fever), skin rash, GI upset. PTU → hepatotoxicity (boxed warning). Methimazole → teratogenic (aplasia cutis) in first trimester.
  • Iodides: metallic taste, sialoadenitis, allergic reactions; prolonged use → escape from Wolff-Chaikoff effect → worsened hyperthyroidism.
  • I-131: hypothyroidism (expected outcome, treat with levothyroxine), radiation thyroiditis, worsening ophthalmopathy in Graves’.
  • Beta-blockers: bradycardia, bronchospasm (avoid non-selective in asthma), fatigue.

Oral exam pearls

  • PTU has two unique features: safe in 1st trimester + blocks peripheral T4→T3
  • Agranulocytosis is the most feared thioamide side effect — check WBC if fever/sore throat
  • Always give thioamide BEFORE iodide in thyroid storm (iodide alone feeds hormone synthesis)
  • I-131 is contraindicated in pregnancy and lactation
Say it aloud — 30–60 sec

Thioamides (methimazole, PTU): Inhibit thyroid peroxidase, blocking hormone synthesis; PTU also blocks peripheral T4→T3. Methimazole is first-line; PTU is preferred in the first trimester and thyroid storm. Iodides: High doses transiently inhibit organification (Wolff-Chaikoff) and reduce gland vascularity before surgery. Radioactive iodine (I-131): Destroys thyroid tissue — definitive treatment, contraindicated in pregnancy. Beta-blockers (propranolol): Control tachycardia and tremor. Adverse effects: Agranulocytosis is the feared thioamide effect (watch for sore throat/fever); PTU causes hepatotoxicity; I-131 causes hypothyroidism.

IV.2Treatment of Hypothyroidism

Definition/Intro

Levothyroxine (synthetic T4) is the drug of choice for hypothyroidism, providing a stable, long-acting source of thyroid hormone.

Mechanism of action

  • Synthetic T4 is converted peripherally to T3 (the active form) by 5’-deiodinase
  • T3 enters the nucleus → binds thyroid hormone receptors → activates gene transcription → increases basal metabolic rate, O2 consumption, and protein synthesis

Pharmacokinetics

  • Oral bioavailability ~80%; absorption decreased by food, calcium, iron, antacids, PPIs — take on an empty stomach, 30–60 min before breakfast
  • Long half-life (~7 days) → once-daily dosing, steady state in 6–8 weeks
  • Highly protein-bound (to TBG, albumin, transthyretin)
  • Monitor with TSH levels (most sensitive); adjust dose every 6–8 weeks

Dosing notes

  • Start low (25–50 mcg/day), especially in elderly and cardiac patients — rapid replacement can precipitate angina or arrhythmia
  • Full replacement dose ~1.6 mcg/kg/day in adults
  • Pregnancy increases dose requirement by ~30–50%

Adverse effects

  • Essentially symptoms of iatrogenic hyperthyroidism if over-dosed: tachycardia, palpitations, tremor, heat intolerance, weight loss, insomnia, diarrhea
  • Cardiac risk: angina, arrhythmias (especially atrial fibrillation in elderly)
  • Chronic excess → accelerated bone loss (osteoporosis), especially in postmenopausal women

Oral exam pearls

  • Levothyroxine must be taken fasting — calcium, iron, and antacids impair absorption
  • Start low, go slow in elderly/cardiac patients
  • TSH is the monitoring parameter; recheck at 6–8 weeks after dose change
  • T4 has a 7-day half-life → takes weeks to reach steady state
Say it aloud — 30–60 sec

Drug and mechanism: Levothyroxine (synthetic T4) is converted peripherally to active T3, which binds nuclear receptors to raise metabolic rate. Pharmacokinetics: ~80% oral absorption, reduced by food, calcium, iron and antacids — take on an empty stomach; long ~7-day half-life, so monitor TSH and adjust every 6–8 weeks; start low in the elderly and cardiac patients; pregnancy raises the requirement. Adverse effects: Essentially over-replacement — palpitations, tremor, heat intolerance, weight loss — and chronic excess causes osteoporosis and atrial fibrillation.

IV.3Insulin and Insulin Analogs

Definition/Intro

Insulin is a peptide hormone that lowers blood glucose; exogenous insulin is essential for type 1 DM and used in advanced type 2 DM.

Mechanism of action

  • Binds the insulin receptor (receptor tyrosine kinase) → autophosphorylation → activates IRS-1 → PI3K/Akt pathway
  • Promotes GLUT4 translocation to cell membrane → increased glucose uptake in muscle and adipose
  • Stimulates glycogenesis, lipogenesis, and protein synthesis
  • Inhibits gluconeogenesis, glycogenolysis, lipolysis, and proteolysis

Pharmacokinetics (types by onset/duration)

Category Examples Onset Peak Duration
Rapid-acting Lispro, Aspart, Glulisine 15–30 min 0.5–1 h 3–5 h
Short-acting Regular insulin 30–60 min 2–3 h 5–8 h
Intermediate NPH (isophane) 2–4 h 4–10 h 10–16 h
Long-acting Glargine, Detemir 1–2 h Peakless* 20–24 h
Ultra-long Degludec 1–2 h Peakless >42 h

*Glargine forms microprecipitates at physiologic pH → slow steady release; Detemir binds albumin for prolonged action.

  • Rapid-acting analogs: amino acid sequence modifications prevent hexamer formation → faster absorption
  • Regular insulin is the only insulin given IV (used in DKA, hyperkalemia)
  • All insulins are given subcutaneously except regular (also IV)

Adverse effects

  • Hypoglycemia — most common and most dangerous; risk highest with tight control, irregular meals, exercise
  • Weight gain — insulin is anabolic
  • Lipodystrophy — lipoatrophy or lipohypertrophy at injection sites (rotate sites)
  • Hypokalemia — insulin drives K+ into cells (therapeutic use in hyperkalemia)
  • Allergic reactions (rare with human recombinant insulin)

Oral exam pearls

  • Rapid-acting analogs = “log” mnemonic (Lispro, Aspart, Glulisine) — inject right before meals
  • Regular insulin is the ONLY IV insulin — know this for DKA and hyperkalemia
  • Glargine is peakless → basal coverage; cannot be mixed with other insulins (acidic pH)
  • Hypoglycemia and weight gain are the two big adverse effects of all insulins
Say it aloud — 30–60 sec

Mechanism: Binds a receptor tyrosine kinase, activating PI3K/Akt and translocating GLUT4 so glucose enters muscle and fat; it also drives glycogen, fat and protein synthesis and pushes potassium into cells. Pharmacokinetics: Rapid-acting (lispro, aspart, glulisine) for meals; regular is the only IV insulin (DKA, hyperkalaemia); NPH is intermediate; glargine and detemir are long-acting and peakless. Adverse effects: Hypoglycaemia (the main danger), weight gain, injection-site lipodystrophy and hypokalaemia.

IV.4Amylin Analogs (Pramlintide)

Definition/Intro

Pramlintide is a synthetic analog of amylin, a peptide co-secreted with insulin from pancreatic beta cells.

Mechanism of action

  • Slows gastric emptying → reduces postprandial glucose spikes
  • Suppresses postprandial glucagon secretion
  • Promotes satiety (acts on CNS) → reduces food intake

Pharmacokinetics

  • Given by subcutaneous injection before meals (cannot be mixed with insulin in the same syringe)
  • Rapid absorption, short duration → mealtime dosing
  • Used as adjunct to insulin in both type 1 and type 2 DM when postprandial control is inadequate

Adverse effects

  • Hypoglycemia — especially in type 1 DM (must reduce mealtime insulin dose by 50% when starting pramlintide)
  • Nausea — most common, dose-limiting; improves with gradual titration
  • GI upset (anorexia, vomiting)

Oral exam pearls

  • Pramlintide = “3 actions”: slow gastric emptying, suppress glucagon, increase satiety
  • Always reduce insulin dose by 50% when starting pramlintide to avoid severe hypoglycemia
  • Nausea is the most common side effect
  • Cannot be mixed in the same syringe as insulin
Say it aloud — 30–60 sec

Mechanism: A synthetic amylin analog that slows gastric emptying, suppresses postprandial glucagon and increases satiety. Pharmacokinetics: Injected subcutaneously before meals; cannot be mixed with insulin in the same syringe. Adverse effects: Nausea (dose-limiting) and hypoglycaemia — reduce mealtime insulin by 50% when starting, especially in type 1 diabetes.

IV.5Sulfonylureas

Definition/Intro

Sulfonylureas are oral insulin secretagogues that stimulate endogenous insulin release; require functioning beta cells.

Mechanism of action

  • Bind the SUR1 subunit of the ATP-sensitive K+ channel on pancreatic beta cells → channel closure → membrane depolarization → Ca2+ influx via voltage-gated Ca2+ channels → insulin exocytosis
  • Action is glucose-independent (secrete insulin regardless of blood glucose level → hypoglycemia risk)

Pharmacokinetics

  • First generation: Chlorpropamide, Tolbutamide (less used, more side effects)
  • Second generation: Glyburide (Glibenclamide), Glipizide, Glimepiride — more potent, fewer drug interactions, preferred
  • Oral, well absorbed; highly protein-bound
  • Hepatic metabolism, renal excretion
  • Glyburide has active metabolites → accumulates in renal impairment → higher hypoglycemia risk
  • Glipizide preferred in elderly/renal impairment (inactive metabolites)

Adverse effects

  • Hypoglycemia — most important; worst with glyburide and chlorpropamide (long duration, active metabolites)
  • Weight gain (insulin is anabolic)
  • Disulfiram-like reaction with alcohol (especially chlorpropamide)
  • Chlorpropamide → SIADH (hyponatremia)
  • Cross-reactivity in sulfa allergy (controversial but tested)

Oral exam pearls

  • MOA = close K-ATP channels independent of glucose → hypoglycemia risk
  • Glyburide is avoided in renal failure; glipizide is safer
  • Chlorpropamide: disulfiram reaction + SIADH — classic exam traps
  • Weight gain distinguishes sulfonylureas from metformin (weight-neutral) and GLP-1 agonists (weight loss)
Say it aloud — 30–60 sec

Mechanism: Bind the SUR1 subunit and close the beta-cell ATP-sensitive potassium channel, causing depolarisation, calcium influx and insulin release — glucose-independent, so they need working beta cells. Pharmacokinetics: Second-generation agents (glyburide, glipizide, glimepiride) are preferred; glyburide has active metabolites and is avoided in renal impairment, glipizide is safer. Adverse effects: Hypoglycaemia (main effect) and weight gain; chlorpropamide causes a disulfiram-like reaction and SIADH.

IV.6Glinides (Repaglinide, Nateglinide)

Definition/Intro

Glinides are short-acting insulin secretagogues, also called meglitinides; they target postprandial hyperglycemia.

Mechanism of action

  • Same mechanism as sulfonylureas — bind the K-ATP channel on beta cells (but at a different site on the SUR1 subunit) → channel closure → depolarization → Ca2+ influx → insulin release
  • Faster onset, shorter duration than sulfonylureas → “meal-time” secretagogues

Pharmacokinetics

  • Repaglinide: more potent; metabolized by CYP3A4 and hepatic glucuronidation; eliminated in bile; can be used in renal impairment
  • Nateglinide: derived from D-phenylalanine; hepatic metabolism; renal excretion of metabolites
  • Both taken 0–30 min before each meal (“skip the meal, skip the dose”)
  • Very short duration (~4 hours) → less fasting hypoglycemia than sulfonylureas

Adverse effects

  • Hypoglycemia — less than sulfonylureas due to short duration, but still possible
  • Weight gain (insulin secretagogue)
  • Repaglinide: drug interactions via CYP3A4 (gemfibrozil contraindicated — severe hypoglycemia)

Oral exam pearls

  • Same MOA as sulfonylureas but different binding site, faster/shorter action
  • “Eat a meal, take a pill” — flexible dosing around meals
  • Repaglinide is safe in renal impairment (biliary excretion)
  • Less hypoglycemia than sulfonylureas due to short half-life
Say it aloud — 30–60 sec

Mechanism: Like sulfonylureas they close the beta-cell K-ATP channel to release insulin, but bind a different site with faster onset and shorter duration — taken before each meal for postprandial control. Pharmacokinetics: Repaglinide is metabolised hepatically and excreted in bile, so it’s safe in renal impairment; skip the dose if the meal is skipped. Adverse effects: Hypoglycaemia (less than sulfonylureas) and weight gain; gemfibrozil is contraindicated with repaglinide.

IV.7Biguanides (Metformin)

Definition/Intro

Metformin is the first-line oral agent for type 2 DM; it is an insulin sensitizer that does not cause hypoglycemia as monotherapy.

Mechanism of action

  • Activates AMP-activated protein kinase (AMPK)
  • Decreases hepatic glucose production (gluconeogenesis) — primary effect
  • Increases insulin sensitivity in peripheral tissues (muscle, adipose)
  • Reduces intestinal absorption of glucose
  • Does NOT stimulate insulin secretion → no hypoglycemia as monotherapy

Pharmacokinetics

  • Oral; not protein-bound; not metabolized — excreted unchanged by the kidneys
  • Half-life ~6 hours; given 2–3 times daily (extended-release: once daily)
  • Contraindicated in significant renal impairment (eGFR <30) → accumulation → lactic acidosis risk
  • Withheld before iodinated contrast procedures (risk of acute kidney injury → metformin accumulation)

Adverse effects

  • GI effects — most common: nausea, diarrhea, abdominal discomfort, metallic taste (dose-related, improve with extended-release formulation)
  • Lactic acidosis — rare but life-threatening; risk increases with renal impairment, hepatic disease, hypoxia, alcohol abuse, sepsis
  • Vitamin B12 deficiency — with chronic use (impairs ileal B12 absorption) → megaloblastic anemia
  • Weight neutral or mild weight loss (advantage over sulfonylureas/insulin)

Oral exam pearls

  • Metformin = AMPK activation → decreased hepatic gluconeogenesis; no hypoglycemia alone
  • Lactic acidosis is rare but the most tested adverse effect — know the contraindications (renal, hepatic, hypoxic states)
  • Check B12 levels with chronic use
  • Weight-neutral/loss distinguishes it from sulfonylureas and insulin
Say it aloud — 30–60 sec

Mechanism: Activates AMP-kinase, mainly decreasing hepatic gluconeogenesis and increasing peripheral insulin sensitivity; it doesn’t stimulate insulin, so no hypoglycaemia alone, and it’s weight-neutral. Pharmacokinetics: Not metabolised — excreted unchanged by the kidneys, so contraindicated when eGFR is below 30 and held before iodinated contrast (lactic acidosis risk). Adverse effects: GI upset (nausea, diarrhoea, metallic taste), rare but serious lactic acidosis, and vitamin B12 deficiency with long-term use.

IV.8Thiazolidinediones

Definition/Intro

Thiazolidinediones (TZDs, “glitazones”) are insulin sensitizers that act via nuclear receptor activation.

Mechanism of action

  • Bind and activate PPARγ (peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor gamma) — a nuclear transcription factor
  • Enhance transcription of genes involved in glucose and lipid metabolism
  • Increase insulin sensitivity in adipose, muscle, and liver
  • Promote adipocyte differentiation → more small, insulin-sensitive adipocytes → improved glucose uptake
  • Decrease hepatic glucose output

Pharmacokinetics

  • Pioglitazone and Rosiglitazone — both oral, once daily
  • Hepatic metabolism (CYP2C8, CYP3A4); high protein binding
  • Slow onset of action (weeks to months for full effect)
  • Pioglitazone: also improves lipid profile (increases HDL, decreases TG)
  • Rosiglitazone: worsens lipid profile (increases LDL); restricted use due to cardiovascular concerns

Adverse effects

  • Weight gain — fluid retention + increased subcutaneous fat
  • Peripheral edema and heart failure exacerbation — contraindicated in NYHA class III–IV heart failure
  • Bone fractures — increased risk, especially in postmenopausal women (PPARγ diverts mesenchymal stem cells to adipocytes instead of osteoblasts)
  • Hepatotoxicity — monitor LFTs (troglitazone was withdrawn for this; pioglitazone/rosiglitazone have rare hepatic effects)
  • Bladder cancer — possible association with pioglitazone (long-term use)
  • Rosiglitazone → increased cardiovascular events (controversial, led to restrictions)

Oral exam pearls

  • MOA = PPARγ agonist → insulin sensitizer (takes weeks to work)
  • Contraindicated in heart failure (fluid retention/edema)
  • Fracture risk in women — classic exam question
  • Troglitazone withdrawn for hepatotoxicity; pioglitazone linked to bladder cancer
Say it aloud — 30–60 sec

Mechanism: Activate the nuclear receptor PPAR-gamma, increasing insulin sensitivity in fat, muscle and liver; onset takes weeks. Pharmacokinetics: Pioglitazone and rosiglitazone are oral, hepatically metabolised; pioglitazone improves the lipid profile. Adverse effects: Fluid retention, oedema and weight gain — contraindicated in heart failure; increased fractures (especially in women); hepatotoxicity risk (troglitazone was withdrawn); and pioglitazone is linked to bladder cancer.

IV.9Alpha-Glucosidase Inhibitors (Acarbose, Miglitol)

Definition/Intro

Acarbose and Miglitol inhibit carbohydrate digestion in the small intestine to reduce postprandial glucose spikes.

Mechanism of action

  • Reversibly inhibit alpha-glucosidase enzymes in the brush border of the small intestine
  • These enzymes cleave complex carbohydrates and disaccharides into absorbable monosaccharides
  • Inhibition → delayed carbohydrate digestion and glucose absorption → blunted postprandial glucose peak
  • Do NOT affect fasting glucose significantly

Pharmacokinetics

  • Taken orally at the start of each meal (must be present with food to work)
  • Acarbose: poorly absorbed; acts locally in the GI tract; metabolized by intestinal bacteria; large doses — minimal hepatic metabolism
  • Miglitol: systemically absorbed; renally excreted unchanged
  • No hypoglycemia as monotherapy (do not stimulate insulin)
  • If hypoglycemia occurs with combination therapy → treat with oral glucose (dextrose), NOT sucrose (sucrose digestion is blocked)

Adverse effects

  • GI effects — flatulence, bloating, abdominal cramps, diarrhea (most common; due to undigested carbohydrates fermented by colonic bacteria)
  • Acarbose at high doses → reversible elevation of hepatic transaminases
  • Contraindicated in inflammatory bowel disease, intestinal obstruction, cirrhosis

Oral exam pearls

  • Must take with first bite of food — “no food, no effect”
  • GI side effects (flatulence) are the #1 reason for poor compliance
  • Treat hypoglycemia with GLUCOSE, not sucrose — classic exam question
  • Targets postprandial glucose; minimal effect on fasting glucose
Say it aloud — 30–60 sec

Mechanism: Acarbose and miglitol reversibly inhibit intestinal alpha-glucosidase, delaying carbohydrate digestion and blunting the post-meal glucose spike — taken with the first bite of each meal. Pharmacokinetics: Acarbose acts locally and is barely absorbed; miglitol is absorbed and renally excreted. Adverse effects: Flatulence, bloating and diarrhoea (limit compliance); no hypoglycaemia alone, but if it occurs on combination therapy treat with glucose, not sucrose.

IV.10Glucocorticoids — Pharmacological Effects and Therapeutic Uses

Pharmacological effects on different organs

  • Metabolic: increase blood glucose (stimulate gluconeogenesis, decrease peripheral glucose uptake → “diabetogenic”); increase protein catabolism; promote lipolysis with fat redistribution (central/truncal obesity, moon face, buffalo hump)
  • Anti-inflammatory: inhibit phospholipase A2 (via lipocortin/annexin-1) → decrease prostaglandins and leukotrienes; stabilize lysosomal membranes; decrease capillary permeability; suppress NF-κB → reduced cytokine production
  • Immunosuppressive: decrease T-lymphocyte proliferation and function; induce lymphocyte apoptosis; reduce circulating eosinophils, basophils, and monocytes; increase circulating neutrophils (demargination — but impaired function)
  • Musculoskeletal: proximal muscle wasting (protein catabolism); decreased bone formation + increased resorption → osteoporosis
  • CNS: euphoria, insomnia, psychosis at high doses
  • Cardiovascular: permissive effect on catecholamines (maintain vascular tone and responsiveness); sodium/water retention (mineralocorticoid activity of some agents)
  • GI: increase gastric acid secretion; decrease mucosal protection → peptic ulcer risk
  • Hematologic: increase RBCs, platelets, neutrophils; decrease lymphocytes, eosinophils, basophils, monocytes (the “LCD” — Lymphocytes, Eosinophils Count Down; neutrophils count up)

Therapeutic uses

  • Replacement therapy: Addison’s disease (primary adrenal insufficiency), congenital adrenal hyperplasia
  • Anti-inflammatory: asthma, allergic rhinitis, dermatitis, inflammatory bowel disease, rheumatoid arthritis
  • Immunosuppressive: organ transplant rejection prevention, autoimmune diseases (SLE, autoimmune hepatitis)
  • Hematologic malignancies: ALL, lymphomas (induce lymphocyte apoptosis)
  • Cerebral edema: dexamethasone for vasogenic edema (brain tumors)
  • Preterm labor: betamethasone/dexamethasone to accelerate fetal lung maturity
  • Adrenal crisis / shock: IV hydrocortisone

Oral exam pearls

  • Anti-inflammatory MOA = inhibit phospholipase A2 via lipocortin → block all eicosanoid synthesis
  • “Diabetogenic” — always think steroid-induced hyperglycemia
  • Neutrophils UP, lymphocytes/eosinophils DOWN — classic CBC pattern
  • Betamethasone for fetal lung maturity; dexamethasone for cerebral edema
Say it aloud — 30–60 sec

Effects on organs: Metabolic — raise blood glucose (gluconeogenesis, diabetogenic) and increase protein and fat breakdown with central fat redistribution. Anti-inflammatory — inhibit phospholipase A2 via lipocortin, cutting prostaglandins and leukotrienes, and suppress NF-kB. Immunosuppressive — reduce T-cells and cause lymphocyte apoptosis; on the blood count neutrophils rise while lymphocytes and eosinophils fall. Therapeutic uses: Replacement in Addison’s, anti-inflammatory in asthma and rheumatoid arthritis, immunosuppression in transplant and autoimmune disease, haematological malignancies, cerebral oedema (dexamethasone), and betamethasone to mature fetal lungs.

IV.11Glucocorticoids — Pharmacokinetics and Adverse Effects

Pharmacokinetics

  • Well absorbed orally; also given IV, IM, topical, inhaled, intra-articular
  • Highly protein-bound to corticosteroid-binding globulin (CBG/transcortin) and albumin
  • Hepatic metabolism (CYP3A4); renal excretion of metabolites
  • Duration-based classification:
  • Short-acting: Hydrocortisone (cortisol) — t½ ~8 h; highest mineralocorticoid activity; used for replacement
  • Intermediate-acting: Prednisone, Prednisolone, Methylprednisolone — t½ ~12–36 h; prednisone is a prodrug (activated to prednisolone in liver)
  • Long-acting: Dexamethasone, Betamethasone — t½ ~36–72 h; highest anti-inflammatory potency; no mineralocorticoid activity; dexamethasone used in suppression tests
  • Potency comparison: Dexamethasone > Methylprednisolone > Prednisolone > Hydrocortisone (anti-inflammatory)
  • Mineralocorticoid activity: Hydrocortisone >> Prednisolone > Methylprednisolone; Dexamethasone = 0

Adverse effects

  • Cushingoid features (chronic use): moon face, buffalo hump, central obesity, purple striae, thin skin, easy bruising
  • Metabolic: hyperglycemia (steroid diabetes), dyslipidemia, negative nitrogen balance (muscle wasting)
  • Musculoskeletal: osteoporosis (most common cause of drug-induced osteoporosis), avascular necrosis of femoral head, growth retardation in children, proximal myopathy
  • Immune: increased susceptibility to infections; reactivation of TB and latent infections; impaired wound healing
  • GI: peptic ulcer disease (especially with concurrent NSAIDs), pancreatitis
  • Cardiovascular: hypertension, sodium/water retention (mineralocorticoid effect), hypokalemia
  • Ophthalmic: posterior subcapsular cataracts, glaucoma (increased IOP)
  • CNS: euphoria, insomnia, psychosis, depression
  • Adrenal suppression: chronic exogenous steroids suppress the HPA axis → acute adrenal crisis if abruptly discontinued → must taper gradually
  • Dermatologic: skin atrophy, striae, acne, hirsutism

Oral exam pearls

  • NEVER stop chronic glucocorticoids abruptly — taper to prevent adrenal crisis
  • Dexamethasone = most potent anti-inflammatory, ZERO mineralocorticoid activity
  • Osteoporosis is the most common serious chronic adverse effect — prophylax with calcium + vitamin D
  • Prednisone is a prodrug → needs hepatic activation to prednisolone (avoid in severe liver failure)
Say it aloud — 30–60 sec

Pharmacokinetics: Classified by duration — hydrocortisone is short-acting with the most mineralocorticoid effect (replacement); prednisone/prednisolone are intermediate (prednisone is a prodrug activated in the liver); dexamethasone and betamethasone are long-acting, most potent, with no mineralocorticoid activity. Metabolised by hepatic CYP3A4. Adverse effects: Cushingoid features, osteoporosis (the main chronic harm), hyperglycaemia, increased infection risk, cataracts and glaucoma, peptic ulcers, and HPA-axis suppression — never stop abruptly, taper to avoid adrenal crisis.
